# Unit 5: Development & Health

## Topics in this unit

- **Lifespan Development**
- **Health**

## Topic 15: Lifespan Development (media + decision literacy)

1. **Lifespan development** is the study of how people change and stay the same across time in **biological**, **cognitive**, and **social-emotional** areas.
2. Development reflects both **biology** and **environment**, so most real outcomes come from their *interaction* rather than a single cause.
3. People develop in **context**, meaning family, culture, schools, and historical time shape what *typical development* looks like and what support is realistic.
4. Early experiences matter, but they do not write an unchangeable destiny, because development includes **plasticity** as well as *constraints* and *risk factors*.
5. **Adolescence** involves major changes in **identity**, relationships, and **self-regulation**, so strong explanations focus on developing control systems and social context rather than on simple *character stories*.
6. Claims about development should be evaluated by asking whether the evidence tracks change **over time** and whether it separates **age effects** from **cohort**, culture, and opportunity.

## Topic 16: Health (media + decision literacy)

1. **Health psychology** uses the **biopsychosocial model** to explain health and illness as a mix of biological factors, **behaviors**, **thoughts**, **emotions**, and **social conditions**.
2. **Stress** is not only a feeling; it is a **body response** that can help in the short term but can be harmful when it becomes *chronic* and keeps the system activated.
3. **Coping strategies** vary in effectiveness, and a useful distinction is whether coping reduces the **problem**, reduces the **emotional impact**, or adds new *costs* later.
4. **Health behaviors** (sleep, activity, substance use, nutrition, adherence) are shaped by **learning**, **environment**, and **habit loops**, not just *willpower* or *knowledge*.
5. **Placebo** and **expectation effects** show that beliefs can change symptoms and outcomes, which is why good health claims require **controlled comparisons** and **replication**.
6. When evaluating health headlines, the key questions are whether the result is **correlational** or **causal**, how big the **effect size** is, and whether *confounds* like access, baseline risk, and stress exposure were addressed.

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 5: Development & Health (media + decision literacy)

1. **Development** is change over time across **biological**, **cognitive**, and **social-emotional** domains, which means “why someone is like this” often requires both history and **context**.
2. **Nature and nurture** interact, so genes influence potential while environments shape what develops, and simple *one-cause stories* usually fail.
3. Early experiences matter, but development includes **plasticity**, so *risk is not destiny* and support can change trajectories.
4. **Health** is best understood through a **biopsychosocial model**, because **stress**, habits, relationships, resources, and biology all contribute to outcomes.
5. **Chronic stress** can alter **sleep**, immune function, mood, and decision making, so burnout and self-control problems can be partly predictable **physiological effects**.
6. The most practical health skill is evaluating claims by checking **study design**, **effect size**, *confounds*, and whether the recommendation is *feasible* and *safe* for real lives.
