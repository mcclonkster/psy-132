### Topic 8: Perception (through the lens of the documents)


**Topic 8 (Perception)** is the process by which the brain **selects, organizes, interprets, and consciously experiences** sensory information. In the documents, perception is explicitly framed as “psychological” in the sense that it is shaped by **attention, expectations, experience, and context**, even though it is built from sensory input. 【turn4:6†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L27-L35】【turn4:3†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L9-L15】

### What “Perception” means in this corpus

#### Perception is built from sensation but is not identical to it

Your module draws a clean boundary:


*   **Sensation** is input detected by receptors.
*   **Perception** is what the brain does with that input: selection, organization, interpretation, and conscious experience. 【turn4:16†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L26-L29】【turn4:6†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L31-L34】


It also explicitly notes that perception of the “same” sensory input can vary between people because interpretation is influenced by learning, memory, emotions, and expectations. 【turn4:16†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L27-L29】

#### Bottom-up and top-down processing are the core explanatory pair

The documents define perception as involving both:


*   **Bottom-up processing:** perceptions are built from sensory input.
*   **Top-down processing:** interpretation is influenced by knowledge, experiences, and thoughts (context effects). 【turn4:6†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L31-L35】


The module uses the classic ambiguous “B/13” style context examples to show how the same stimulus can be perceived differently when context changes. 【turn4:3†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L1-L10】

#### Attention determines what is perceived

Your module explicitly elevates attention from “extra detail” to a determining mechanism:


*   Attention affects what is **sensed versus perceived**, with everyday examples (cocktail party effect).
*   The Simons and Chabris selective attention study illustrates **inattentional blindness** (many participants fail to notice the gorilla while counting passes). 【turn4:3†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L25-L41】

### How perception is organized in these documents

#### Gestalt principles as the “organization rules” for perception

The documents treat Gestalt psychology as a major framework for how perception organizes inputs into meaningful wholes:


*   Gestalt claims perception is more than simply combining stimuli; the whole is different from the sum of parts.
*   The module emphasizes predictable organizational principles, including:
    *   **Figure-ground**
    *   **Proximity**
    *   **Similarity**
    *   **Continuity (good continuation)**
    *   **Closure** 【turn4:2†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L6-L13】【turn4:0†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L18-L26】


It also introduces **perceptual hypotheses** and **perceptual set** as “educated guesses” shaped by expectations and experience, which explains why perception can diverge from physical input. 【turn4:8†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L29-L33】

#### Depth perception as an example of inference from cues

Perception is shown as inference-making, not passive recording:


*   **Depth perception** is defined as perceiving spatial relationships in 3-D space.
*   Depth is constructed using:
    *   **Binocular cues** (for example, binocular disparity)
    *   **Monocular cues** (for example, linear perspective, interposition, relative size, light/shadow) 【turn4:13†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L16-L35】【turn4:10†05\_Sensation and Perception.pdf†L2-L5】

### Topic 8: Perception

### Arc function

Perception defines the **interpretation layer**: how the brain organizes sensation into experienced reality.

### Conceptual movement

*   From “seeing is believing” to **perception as constructed inference**
*   From bottom-up input to **bottom-up + top-down** processing (context, expectations, attention)

### Learning emphasis

*   Organization rules (Gestalt principles) and inference rules (depth cues, constancies).
*   Attention as a gating mechanism (inattentional/change blindness) that constrains what enters awareness.

### Student-facing payoff (@jalcstudent)

This answers: “Why do people disagree about what they saw?” and supplies a non-moral explanation: the system is built to infer, not to record.
