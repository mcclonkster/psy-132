# Unit 8: Disorders & Treatment

## Topics in this unit

- **Abnormal**
- **Therapies**

## Topic 23: Abnormal (media + decision literacy)

1. **Abnormal psychology** studies patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that are unusually *distressing* or *impairing*, and “abnormal” is judged using ideas like **distress**, **dysfunction**, **danger**, and **deviance** while keeping **culture** and **context** in view.
2. Diagnostic categories like those in the **DSM** are tools for **communication** and **treatment planning**, not identities and not perfect natural types of people.
3. Many symptoms exist on a **continuum**, so cutoffs for a diagnosis are often practical decisions about **severity**, **duration**, and **impairment** rather than a clean *present versus absent* boundary.
4. Most disorders are best understood with a **biopsychosocial model**, where **biology**, **psychology**, and **social context** interact.
5. Overlapping symptoms are common (**comorbidity**), so good **assessment** looks at **timing**, **triggers**, medical causes, substance effects, and **functional impact** before concluding what is going on.
6. When distress or impairment is *persistent*, or when **safety** is a concern, the appropriate next step is prompt support from trusted adults and qualified professionals rather than **self-diagnosis** based on a checklist.

## Topic 24: Therapies (media + decision literacy)

1. Treatments should be judged by **evidence**, meaning improved outcomes compared with appropriate comparison groups, because improvement can also happen through **placebo effects**, **regression toward the mean**, or *time*.
2. **Psychotherapy** includes multiple approaches, but many **evidence-based therapies** share structured skills practice, and **CBT** often targets patterns in thoughts and behaviors that maintain distress.
3. For some problems, specific techniques matter a lot; for example, **exposure** is a central, evidence-based method for many anxiety problems, so talking about a fear is not the same as practicing a tested **change process**.
4. **Medications** can reduce symptoms by changing **brain signaling**, but benefits, side effects, and withdrawal risks vary by person, and medication is often strongest when paired with **skills** and **support** rather than used as the only tool.
5. The **therapeutic alliance** (*trust*, *collaboration*, *shared goals*) predicts outcomes across many therapies, but a strong relationship does not replace competent methods, clear boundaries, and **ethical practice**.
6. A smart way to evaluate therapy claims is to look for **licensed**, transparent providers who explain risks and evidence, avoid *guaranteed cures*, and use ongoing **measurement** to adjust the plan when progress stalls.

## Unit 8: Disorders & Treatment (media + decision literacy)

1. **Psychological disorders** are patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that involve significant **distress** or **impairment**, and these judgments must be made with **culture** and **context** in view.
2. Diagnostic labels such as those in the **DSM** are tools for **communication** and **planning**, not fixed identities and not perfect categories of human nature.
3. Many symptoms exist on a **continuum**, so **severity**, **duration**, and **functioning** often matter more than a simple *“has it or does not”* frame.
4. Explanations and treatments are strongest when they use a **biopsychosocial model** that includes **biology**, **learning**, thinking patterns, relationships, and real-world **stressors**.
5. Treatment claims should be judged by **evidence**, appropriate **comparisons**, meaningful **effect sizes**, and **replication**, because improvement can happen for reasons unrelated to the treatment itself.
6. High-quality care includes competent methods, **ethical boundaries**, and ongoing **progress checks**, and it avoids *guaranteed cures*, stigma-driven narratives, and one-size-fits-all explanations.
