# Unit 7: Social & Identity

## Topics in this unit

- **Social**
- **Personality**
- **Gender**
- **Multicultural**

## Topic 19: Social (media + decision literacy)

1. **Social psychology** studies how people think about, influence, and relate to one another, which is why behavior often changes when an *audience*, a **group**, or a **norm** is present.
2. **Social influence** shows up as **conformity**, **compliance**, and **obedience**, and small situational pressures can produce big behavior shifts without anyone feeling *“controlled.”*
3. We explain behavior using **attributions**, but the **fundamental attribution error** makes us over-credit **personality** and under-credit **situation** when judging other people.
4. **Groups** can improve decisions when they add *diverse information*, but they can also distort judgment through **groupthink**, **polarization**, and **diffusion of responsibility**.
5. **Prejudice** (*attitudes*), **stereotypes** (*beliefs*), and **discrimination** (*behavior*) are related but not identical, which helps explain why *good intentions* do not automatically prevent harm.
6. To evaluate a claim about “people are just like that,” ask what **incentives**, **norms**, **role expectations**, and immediate *constraints* were shaping behavior in that moment.

## Topic 20: Personality (media + decision literacy)

1. **Personality** describes relatively stable patterns in thinking, feeling, and behaving, but those patterns still shift across **contexts** and over time.
2. **Trait approaches**, especially the **Big Five**, summarize common dimensions people differ on, which can be useful for prediction without pretending traits explain everything.
3. The **person–situation debate** matters because behavior is often best predicted by patterns across situations rather than by a single label like *introvert* or *lazy*.
4. **Psychodynamic**, **humanistic**, and **social-cognitive** approaches disagree about what drives personality (*unconscious conflict*, *growth needs*, *learned expectations*), which is a reminder that theories are **models**, not *x-rays*.
5. Personality measurement only earns trust when it demonstrates **reliability** (*consistency*) and **validity** (*measuring what it claims*), because a test can be consistent and still measure the wrong thing.
6. A practical way to use personality science is to treat traits as **probabilities**, then look for situational **supports** that make the best behavior easier and the worst behavior harder.

## Topic 21: Gender (media + decision literacy)

1. In psychology, it helps to distinguish **sex** (*biological attributes*), **gender identity** (*internal sense of gender*), and **gender expression** (*how someone presents*), because they are related but not the same.
2. **Gender roles** and **stereotypes** are learned through **socialization**, media, and institutions, which is why “normal” gender behavior varies across cultures and historical time.
3. Average group differences between men and women are often smaller than people assume, and there is substantial **overlap**, so single stories about *“male brains”* and *“female brains”* usually overreach.
4. Gender development reflects both **biological influences** and **social influences**, which makes simple one-cause explanations unreliable.
5. **Bias** and **stereotype threat** can change performance and self-concept in real time, which is one reason measured differences can reflect context as much as ability.
6. When evaluating a gender claim, ask what is being **measured**, how big the **effect** is, how groups are **defined**, and whether alternative explanations like opportunity, stress, and expectations were tested.

## Topic 22: Multicultural (media + decision literacy)

1. **Culture** shapes perception, values, emotion norms, and social behavior, so human nature is always filtered through *learned meanings* and *shared practices*.
2. People can be socialized into different orientations like **individualism** and **collectivism**, which affects how they explain success, responsibility, and relationships.
3. **Ethnocentrism** (*treating your culture as the default*) creates blind spots, so cultural psychology uses **cultural relativism** to interpret behavior within its **context** without excusing harm.
4. **Acculturation** and **bicultural identity** show that adapting to a new cultural environment can change **stress**, belonging, and mental health, especially when **discrimination** or language barriers are present.
5. Psychological research must check for **cultural bias** in sampling and measurement, including whether findings from **WEIRD** populations generalize to everyone else.
6. A strong multicultural lens asks not only “What does this person believe?” but also “What **systems**, **identities**, and lived experiences are shaping their options and risks right now?”

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 7: Social & Identity (media + decision literacy)

1. **Social psychology** shows that behavior is strongly shaped by **situations**, **norms**, roles, and **incentives**, even when people feel like they are acting freely.
2. **Social influence** takes recognizable forms such as **conformity**, **compliance**, and **obedience**, so pressure can work without force and without anyone naming it.
3. **Attribution errors**, especially the **fundamental attribution error**, lead people to over-explain others by **personality** and under-explain them by **context**, which reliably fuels conflict and misjudgment.
4. **Personality traits** describe stable tendencies, but they predict best when treated as **probabilities** that interact with environments rather than as permanent labels or destiny claims.
5. **Gender** and **culture** shape expectations, opportunities, and stress exposure, so differences people attribute to nature often include **socialization**, **bias**, and unequal conditions.
6. A strong identity lens asks both “What does this person choose?” and “What options are realistically available given belonging, safety, resources, and **social power**?”
