## Segment guide with integrated instructor notes

### Segment A: What psychology is

- Student main ideas
  - Psychology studies behavior and mental processes
  - Psychology aims to understand and predict behavior using evidence-based approaches
- Student write-down line
  - Psychology is the scientific study of behavior and mental processes.
- Instructor talk move
  - Elicit one behavior example and one mental-process example, then categorize
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Which is easier to observe directly, behavior or mental processes, and what does psychology do about that
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Mental processes are inferred from evidence and measured through methods rather than assumed from intuition

### Segment B: Why psychology is a science

- Student main ideas
  - Personal experience can feel convincing while still being misleading
  - Scientific approaches reduce error by using systematic observation and testing
- Student write-down line
  - Science uses systematic methods to build and test explanations and reduce bias and error.
- Instructor talk move
  - Prompt a confident claim about behavior, then ask what evidence would change the claim
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What counts as disconfirming evidence in psychology
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Confidence and anecdote have low diagnostic value without systematic methods

### Segment C: Early perspectives and what they prioritized

#### Structuralism

- Student main ideas
  - Structuralism aimed to identify the elements of conscious experience
  - Introspection was used to report experience in a structured way
  - Reliability limits follow from subjectivity and variability across people
- Student write-down line
  - Structuralism focused on the components of conscious experience, often using introspection.
- Instructor talk move
  - Run a brief introspection demonstration and compare variability across students
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What reliability problems appear when a method depends on subjective report
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Introspection can generate useful data, but reliability constraints limit what it can support on its own

#### Functionalism

- Student main ideas
  - Functionalism focused on what mental processes and behavior do
  - Emphasis on function and adaptation in context
- Student write-down line
  - Functionalism focused on the function of behavior and mental processes.
- Instructor talk move
  - Use anxiety as an example and separate possible function from harmful intensity
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - How can the same process support adaptation in one context and impair functioning in another
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Function language can be misread as moral approval or universal benefit, so the context boundary must be explicit

#### Psychoanalysis

- Student main ideas
  - Psychoanalysis emphasized unconscious influences and internal conflict
  - Historically influential and also debated
- Student write-down line
  - Psychoanalysis emphasizes unconscious processes that can influence thoughts and behavior.
- Instructor talk move
  - Use a neutral example of nonconscious influence on attention or choice
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What does a mental process look like when it influences behavior outside awareness
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Unconscious processes can be described as mental activity outside awareness, without supernatural framing

#### Behaviorism

- Student main ideas
  - Behaviorism emphasized observable behavior and measurable environmental influences
  - Emphasis on measurement and testing
- Student write-down line
  - Behaviorism focuses on observable behavior and how it is shaped by learning and environment.
- Instructor talk move
  - Rewrite mentalistic explanations into observable behavior descriptions
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What is gained and what is lost when explanations emphasize only observable behavior
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Behavior-focused methods prioritize measurement and observability; they do not require claims about the nonexistence of mental life

### Segment D: Integration and modern psychology

- Student main ideas
  - Psychology includes multiple perspectives because behavior is complex
  - Different perspectives use different tools and answer different questions
- Student write-down line
  - Different perspectives answer different questions and use different tools.
- Instructor talk move
  - Present one research question and ask which perspective fits best and why
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Which level of explanation best fits a given question, biological, cognitive, behavioral, or social
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Multiple perspectives imply multiple tools, not relativism

## Quick check for Video 1

- Prompt
  - Choose one perspective and answer both
    - What does it focus on
    - What does it tend to ignore or downplay
- Scoring note
  - Priorities and limits are required for full credit
  - Name-only responses do not meet the target

## Segment guide with integrated instructor notes

### Segment A: Why research methods matter

- Student main ideas
  - Humans show predictable thinking errors
  - Research methods are designed to reduce those errors
- Student write-down line
  - Scientific methods help reduce bias and error in how we interpret experience.
- Instructor talk move
  - Elicit a hindsight-bias example and label it explicitly
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Why does hindsight bias make weak evidence feel convincing
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Post-outcome certainty has low value as evidence about prediction

### Segment B: Scientific method basics in psychology

- Student main ideas
  - Theories organize observations and help explain patterns
  - Hypotheses are testable predictions derived from theories
  - Operational definitions specify how variables are measured or manipulated
  - Replication increases confidence in findings
- Student write-down lines
  - A hypothesis is a testable prediction.
  - An operational definition tells how a concept is measured.
- Instructor talk move
  - Operationalize a construct in two measurable ways, then compare implications
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - How can different operational definitions change what a study concludes
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Theory, hypothesis, and operational definition differ in function and level

### Segment C: Descriptive methods

#### Case studies

- Student main ideas
  - Case studies provide deep detail about one case
  - They can generate hypotheses and illustrate phenomena
  - They rarely justify broad generalization on their own
- Student write-down line
  - Case studies are rich in detail but limited for generalizing to a population.
- Instructor talk move
  - Contrast generate hypotheses with test hypotheses using an example
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Why does vividness inflate perceived evidentiary strength
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Representativeness and generalizability remain unresolved in a single case

#### Naturalistic observation

- Student main ideas
  - Naturalistic observation describes behavior in real settings without manipulation
  - It can reveal patterns and contexts of behavior
  - It cannot establish cause and effect by itself
- Student write-down line
  - Naturalistic observation can describe behavior and patterns, but it cannot establish causation.
- Instructor talk move
  - Provide an observation vignette and require multiple plausible explanations
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What design change would allow stronger causal inference
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Realism and causal inference are different design goals

### Segment D: Self-report methods

- Student main ideas
  - Surveys and interviews collect self-reported attitudes, beliefs, and experiences
  - Results depend on wording, response bias, and sampling
  - Random sampling supports generalization to a population
- Student write-down lines
  - Random sampling supports generalization.
  - Random assignment supports causal inference.
- Instructor talk move
  - Compare two wordings of the same question and predict how results change
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Which bias risks appear in self-report methods
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Random sampling and random assignment have different purposes and different effects

### Segment E: Correlational research

- Student main ideas
  - Correlation describes an association between variables
  - Correlation can support prediction
  - Correlation does not establish causation
  - Directionality and third variables remain unresolved
- Student write-down line
  - Correlation does not prove causation.
- Instructor talk move
  - Require a third-variable explanation for a correlation claim
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Which threats to causal inference remain in correlational research
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Association and causation operate at different inferential levels

### Segment F: Experiments and causal inference

- Student main ideas
  - Experiments manipulate an independent variable
  - Experiments measure effects on a dependent variable
  - Control groups support comparison
  - Placebos and blinding can reduce expectancy effects in many contexts
- Student write-down lines
  - Independent variable is manipulated.
  - Dependent variable is measured.
- Instructor talk move
  - Identify independent and dependent variables in a scenario, then add a control condition
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - Why does random assignment support causal inference
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Two groups do not define an experiment unless manipulation and appropriate control logic are present

### Segment G: Proper research practices and ethics preview

- Student main ideas
  - Study design requires clear variables, procedures, and comparison logic
  - Replication strengthens confidence in findings
  - Informed consent is a baseline participant protection
- Student write-down line
  - Ethics is part of study design, not an afterthought.
- Instructor talk move
  - Use a worked-example template for students to reuse
- Instructor discussion prompt
  - What evidence pattern would increase confidence in a claim
- Instructor misconception correction
  - Converging evidence and replication reduce the risk of overgeneralizing from one result

## Quick formative check for Video 2

- Prompt
  - Given one research question, choose the best method and state both
    - the strongest justified conclusion
    - the key limit
- Methods
  - case study
  - naturalistic observation
  - survey or interview
  - correlational study
  - experiment
