# Week 4 Exam 1 Review Companion

## What this is

This is a review guide for Exam 1, aligned to Units 1 and 2, Topics 1 through 9.

Use it to:

- check your understanding of each topic
- practice retrieval without looking at notes
- focus on the distinctions that show up most often on exams

## How to study with this document

- Step 1
  - Try the quick checks without looking at notes
- Step 2
  - If you miss something, open your Week 1–3 companions and fix your answer
- Step 3
  - Repeat until you can explain each idea in your own words

## What you are responsible for on Exam 1

- Topic 1 History
  - why psychology became a science
  - what early movements emphasized, at a basic level
- Topic 2 Theoretical Perspectives
  - what a perspective prioritizes
  - what a perspective tends to ignore or downplay
- Topic 3 Research Methods
  - what each method can conclude
  - correlation versus causation
  - experiment basics
- Topic 4 Statistics
  - basic interpretation ideas used in research reasoning
  - sampling and representativeness as concepts
- Topic 5 Ethics
  - the purpose of research ethics
  - informed consent as a baseline and what it is for
- Topic 6 Neuroscience
  - neuron communication basics
  - central versus peripheral nervous system
  - broad brain-structure and function links without oversimplifying
- Topic 7 Sensation
  - sensation versus perception
  - transduction, thresholds, adaptation
- Topic 8 Perception
  - bottom-up and top-down processing
  - context and expectations
  - why illusions matter
- Topic 9 Consciousness
  - consciousness as awareness and attention
  - sleep and circadian rhythm basics
  - REM and NREM at a high level

# Exam 1 review checklist by topic

## Topic 1 History

- Quick check
  - In two to three sentences, explain why psychology is treated as a science rather than only opinion.
- Quick check
  - Name one early school of thought and state what it focused on.

## Topic 2 Theoretical Perspectives

- Quick check
  - Choose one perspective and answer both.
    - What does it focus on.
    - What does it tend to ignore or downplay.
- Quick check
  - Explain why multiple perspectives can coexist in psychology without meaning anything goes.

## Topic 3 Research Methods

- Quick check
  - Distinguish these method types using one sentence each.
    - case study
    - naturalistic observation
    - survey or interview
    - correlational study
    - experiment
- Quick check
  - Explain why correlation does not establish causation using either directionality or third-variable logic.

## Topic 4 Statistics

- Quick check
  - Explain what representativeness means and why it matters for surveys.
- Quick check
  - Distinguish random sampling from random assignment.

## Topic 5 Ethics

- Quick check
  - In two sentences, explain what informed consent is for.
- Quick check
  - Explain why ethics is part of research design rather than an optional add-on.

## Topic 6 Neuroscience

- Quick check
  - In two to three sentences, explain how neurons communicate.
- Quick check
  - Distinguish central nervous system from peripheral nervous system.

## Topic 7 Sensation

- Quick check
  - Define sensation and perception in one sentence each.
- Quick check
  - Define transduction.
- Quick check
  - Distinguish absolute threshold from difference threshold.

## Topic 8 Perception

- Quick check
  - Give one example each of bottom-up processing and top-down processing.
- Quick check
  - Explain why illusions are useful evidence about perception.

## Topic 9 Consciousness

- Quick check
  - Define consciousness in one sentence.
- Quick check
  - Explain circadian rhythm in two sentences.
- Quick check
  - State one difference between REM and NREM sleep.

# Exam-style practice prompts

Use these as self-test prompts. Answer out loud or in writing.

- A study shows that people who sleep less report more stress. What can the study conclude and what can it not conclude.
- Two people witness the same event and give different accounts. Give one perception-based explanation that does not assume lying.
- A researcher assigns participants randomly to caffeine or no caffeine before a task. What makes this closer to an experiment than a correlational study.
- You stop noticing a smell after being in a room for ten minutes. Which concept explains this and why.

# Common confusions to avoid

- Confusion
  - Correlation proves causation
- Confusion
  - Random sampling and random assignment are interchangeable
- Confusion
  - One brain region equals one behavior in a simple one-to-one way
- Confusion
  - Sensation and perception are the same thing
- Confusion
  - Top-down processing means making things up
- Confusion
  - Sleep is passive and the brain is inactive
