Thinking and Cognition

Exploring how we process information, solve problems, and organize our mental world.

What is Cognition?

Simply put, cognition is thinking. It encompasses processes associated with perception, knowledge, problem solving, judgment, language, and memory.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between concepts and prototypes.
  • Explain natural vs. artificial concepts.
  • Describe problem-solving strategies (algorithms & heuristics).

Categories and Concepts

A category is a set of objects that can be treated as equivalent. The mental representations we form of categories are called concepts.

Natural Concepts

Created through direct or indirect experience. Examples: Snow, birds, "roughness".

Artificial Concepts

Defined by a specific set of characteristics. Examples: Squares, mathematical formulas.

Problem-Solving Strategies

Method Description Example
Trial and Error Trying solutions until it works. Restarting a phone to fix a glitch.
Algorithm Step-by-step formula. Manual for installing software.
Heuristic General framework; mental shortcuts. Breaking a task into small steps.

Glossary: Cognition

Prototype: The best example of a concept.

Schema: A mental construct of related concepts.

Role Schema: Assumptions about behaviors in certain roles.

Functional Fixedness: Inability to see new uses for objects.

Intelligence and Creativity

What does it mean to be "smart"? Understanding theories, measurement, and potential.

Theories of Intelligence

Analytical

Academic problem solving and computations.

Creative

Imaginative and innovative solutions.

Practical

Street smarts and common sense.

— Robert Sternberg's Triarchic Theory

The Bell Curve

The average IQ score is 100. Standard deviations (usually 15 points) describe how scores are dispersed.

  • 85–115: Considered Average (82% of population)
  • 130+: Considered Gifted
  • Below 70: Intellectual Disability criteria

Creativity: Three Criteria

1

Originality

The idea must have a low probability or be unique.

2

Usefulness

The idea should be valuable or solve a specific problem.

3

Surprise

The idea should be non-obvious to the viewer.

Language and Communication

How words and systematic rules shape human connection and thought.

Components of Language

Lexicon Vocabulary of a given language.
Phoneme Basic sound unit (e.g., "ah").
Semantics How we derive meaning from words.
Syntax How words are organized into sentences.

Language Development Stages

0–3 months Reflexive communication
12–18 months First words stage
18–24 months Simple two-word sentences
3–5 years Complex sentences & conversations

Overgeneralization

Children extending a rule to an exception (e.g., "three mouses" instead of "three mice"). This shows they have understood the rule!

Language and Thought

Does language determine thought? The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis suggests that the language we use determines our patterns of thought.

"Language may indeed influence the way that we think, an idea known as linguistic determinism."

Research on color perception (English vs. Dani people) and time perception (English vs. Mandarin) provides evidence that language can influence how we focus on or organize concepts, though it may not completely determine them.
