**Topic 9 (Consciousness)** is the study of **awareness and its variations**: how people are aware of **external stimuli** (the environment) and **internal stimuli** (thoughts, feelings, bodily states), how that awareness **fluctuates across states** (wakefulness, sleep, dreaming, intoxication, hypnosis, meditation), and how much mental processing occurs **outside** what a person experiences as conscious awareness.

#### Biological rhythms are treated as a major driver of consciousness shifts

The module positions daily changes in consciousness as tightly linked to **biological rhythms**, especially:


*   **Circadian rhythms** (about 24 hours), coordinated by the **suprachiasmatic nucleus (SCN)** and regulated by light-based cues (zeitgebers).
    04\_States of Consciousness
*   Disruptions such as **jet lag** and **rotating shift work**, and the concept of **sleep debt** with cognitive, emotional, and health consequences.
    04\_States of Consciousness


This is one of the module’s strongest “throughlines”: consciousness is not merely philosophical; it is patterned by biology and environment.

#### Conscious awareness is selective and limited

Crash Course #8 makes selective attention the practical “awake consciousness” mechanism:


*   **Selective attention** as the spotlight-like focusing of consciousness on some stimuli while filtering others.

*   **Inattentional blindness** and **change blindness** as evidence that people are less aware of their environments than they believe, even when fully awake.

### Topic 9: Consciousness

### Arc function

Consciousness introduces the **state regulation layer**: awareness varies across states and is reliably limited even when awake.

### Conceptual movement

*   From “consciousness is just being awake” to **consciousness as stateful and constrained**
*   From introspection as authority to **indirect measures and correlates** (EEG patterns, sleep architecture, attention limits)

### Learning emphasis

*   Sleep as structured stages (NREM/REM) tied to measurable brain activity.
*   Circadian regulation as a biological driver of cognitive performance and well-being.
*   Altered states as legitimate phenomena with bounded interpretations (drugs, hypnosis, meditation), paired with myth control.

### Unit-level throughline

Unit 3 positions the learner as an **adaptive system**. Experience changes future behavior through learning, and experience becomes usable knowledge through memory. The unit’s central disciplinary claim is that “who a person is” at any moment reflects **acquisition mechanisms** (conditioning, reinforcement, modeling) plus **storage/retrieval mechanisms** (encoding, consolidation, retrieval cues). The practical consequence is epistemic: confidence and intention do not guarantee accuracy, because both learning and memory operate through predictable rules and predictable error.

### Signature competence for Unit 3

By the end of Unit 3, students can perform a **mechanism-to-prediction workflow**:


*   identify which learning process is operating (classical, operant, observational)
*   predict behavior change based on contingencies (reinforcement/punishment, schedules, extinction, generalization)
*   explain memory outcomes using encoding–storage–retrieval logic
*   evaluate common claims about “habit,” “forgetting,” and “eyewitness accuracy” using reconstructive and interference principles
