### Common misconceptions

- Ethics is only paperwork and slows down real science.
- Informed consent is just a signature on a form.
- If a study is approved by an oversight board, it is automatically ethical in practice.
- Deception is always unethical, or deception is fine whenever it helps research.
- Participants must be told the full purpose immediately in all studies, so any withholding is unethical.
- Confidentiality and anonymity mean the same thing.
- Minimal risk means no risk.
- Offering incentives is always coercive, or incentives cannot create pressure.
- Animal research is either always unethical or always justified if it helps science.
- Historical cases are the main reason ethics exists, rather than ongoing design and conduct choices.

## Big ideas

- Ethics as protection of real people, not just rule-following
- Informed consent as meaningful understanding, not a signature
- Risk and benefit as a balance that must be justified
- Confidentiality and anonymity as different privacy promises
- Debriefing as a safeguard when deception or distress is possible
## Common misconceptions

- Consent makes any study ethical
- Confidentiality and anonymity are the same
- Deception is always acceptable or always forbidden
- Ethics is just personal preference
- If the goal is good, the method is automatically ethical
## Key terms

- **Informed consent**: Agreement to participate after understanding what will happen and what the risks are.
- **Risk**: The possibility of harm, such as stress, loss of privacy, or physical danger.
- **Benefit**: A potential gain, such as knowledge, treatment, or practical improvement.
- **Confidentiality**: Keeping participant information private and limiting who can access it.
- **Anonymity**: No identifying information is collected, so data cannot be linked to a person.
- **Deception**: Withholding information or providing incomplete information about a study on purpose.
- **Debriefing**: Explaining the study afterward, especially if deception was used.
- **IRB**: An institutional review board that evaluates research ethics and participant protection.
- **Coercion**: Pressure that makes participation feel not truly voluntary.
- **Vulnerable population**: A group that needs extra protection because of power or consent limitations.
