# Unit 1: Foundations of Psychological Science

<!-- GENERATED:UNIT-BRIEF-START -->
## Unit study brief

### Big ideas
- Psychology is a science: claims about behavior and mental processes are testable and revisable.
- Theories and perspectives are lenses; the point is explanatory fit and evidence, not loyalty.
- Research methods determine what you can conclude (especially about causation).
- Statistics summarize patterns and uncertainty; they do not create causation by themselves.
- Ethics is risk, consent, privacy, and power—not personal taste.

### Common traps
- Treating common sense as evidence.
- Using correlation language as if it proves causation.
- Confusing random sampling with random assignment.
- Overgeneralizing results beyond the studied group.
- Treating ethics as “they agreed, so it’s fine.”

### Key terms
- **Operational definition**: The specific way a variable is measured or manipulated.
- **Correlation**: Association between variables; does not establish cause.
- **Experiment**: Design that manipulates an independent variable to test causal effects.
- **Random sampling**: Selecting participants to represent a population.
- **Random assignment**: Assigning participants to conditions by chance to reduce group differences.
- **Mean**: Average value; can hide important variation.
- **Variability**: Spread of scores; tells you consistency and risk.
- **Informed consent**: Agreement after understanding procedures and risks.
- **Confidentiality**: Identities protected; researcher can link data to people.
- **Anonymity**: No identifying link exists between data and people.

### Key examples
- **Headline claim audit**: science vs common sense; causal overreach | Student move: State what the claim is and what design would be needed to support it.
- **Sampling vs assignment mini-case**: generalization vs causation | Student move: Label which is missing and what that limits.
- **Average without spread**: mean can mislead | Student move: Ask for variability or distribution before concluding.

### Key studies
- **Milgram obedience paradigm**: Authority context can strongly shape behavior. | Why it matters: Connects methods, inference, and ethics in one famous paradigm.
- **Asch conformity paradigm**: Group pressure can shift judgments even when answers are obvious. | Why it matters: Shows why intuition about behavior is unreliable without evidence.

### Activities
- **Correlation vs experiment sort**: Sort blurbs and write the strongest valid conclusion for each. | Evidence: Discrimination of design → warranted conclusion.
- **Ethics safeguard pitch**: Choose one safeguard for a risky scenario and justify it. | Evidence: One-step application of ethics principles.
