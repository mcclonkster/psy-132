### Common misconceptions

- A theoretical perspective is a personal opinion rather than an explanatory framework that guides evidence and prediction.
- Perspectives are mutually exclusive, so you must pick one “correct” lens for every behavior.
- A perspective is the same thing as a topic area, so “memory” or “emotion” is a perspective.
- Biological explanations imply inevitability or a simple “chemical imbalance” story.
- Behavioral explanations deny thoughts and feelings rather than focusing on what can be observed and measured.
- Cognitive explanations are just introspection and cannot be studied scientifically.
- Psychodynamic explanations are automatically unscientific or, alternatively, automatically true because they are deep.
- Humanistic explanations are motivational slogans rather than a framework that emphasizes meaning, values, and self-concept.
- Sociocultural explanations deny individual differences or personal agency.
- Evolutionary explanations are unfalsifiable storytelling rather than hypothesis-driven accounts with testable predictions.
- Using multiple perspectives means “anything goes,” so evidence and method do not matter.

## Big ideas

- Perspectives as tools that highlight different causes of behavior
- Multiple perspectives fitting the same situation in different ways
- Biopsychosocial thinking as a practical habit
- Limits and boundary conditions for any single explanation
- Choosing interventions based on the best-fitting level of explanation
## Common misconceptions

- One perspective is always the correct explanation
- Biological explanations mean behavior cannot change
- Behavioral explanations ignore thinking completely
- Cognitive explanations mean emotions do not matter
- A perspective is the same thing as a fact
## Key terms

- **Theoretical perspective**: A framework that guides what causes you focus on when explaining behavior.
- **Levels of analysis**: Different layers of explanation, such as biological, psychological, and social.
- **Biological perspective**: Explains behavior using the brain, body, genes, and physiology.
- **Behavioral perspective**: Explains behavior using learning, reinforcement, and conditioning.
- **Cognitive perspective**: Explains behavior using thoughts, attention, memory, and interpretation.
- **Psychodynamic perspective**: Explains behavior using unconscious processes and early experiences.
- **Humanistic perspective**: Emphasizes meaning, values, growth, and personal goals.
- **Sociocultural perspective**: Emphasizes the role of culture, norms, and social context.
- **Evolutionary perspective**: Emphasizes how traits may have developed because they helped survival or reproduction.
- **Biopsychosocial model**: An approach that integrates biological, psychological, and social influences.
