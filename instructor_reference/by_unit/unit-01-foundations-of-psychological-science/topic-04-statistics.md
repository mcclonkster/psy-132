### Common misconceptions

- An average describes what is typical for every individual.
- A statistically significant result means the effect is large or important.
- A non-significant result means there is no effect.
- Correlation implies causation.
- A strong correlation proves a direct causal relationship.
- If two variables correlate, no other variables matter.
- Graphs are objective and cannot be misleading.
- If a scatterplot looks random, there is no relationship worth considering.
- Outliers should always be removed because they are errors.
- Numbers speak for themselves without context, measurement details, or sampling limits.
- Illusory correlation means a person is irrational rather than showing a predictable pattern-seeking tendency.

## Big ideas

- Descriptive statistics as summaries, not full explanations
- The mean as a summary that can hide important variation
- Variability as information about spread and consistency
- Graph literacy as evidence literacy
- Cautious conclusions that match what the data can support
## Common misconceptions

- The mean tells the whole story
- Variability is just error and can be ignored
- A graph trend proves causation
- A small difference is always meaningful
- If data are numerical, conclusions are automatically objective
## Key terms

- **Mean**: The arithmetic average of a set of scores.
- **Median**: The middle score when values are ordered.
- **Mode**: The most frequent value in a set.
- **Variability**: How spread out scores are, not just where the average falls.
- **Standard deviation**: A common measure of how much scores typically differ from the mean.
- **Distribution**: The overall pattern of scores, including shape and spread.
- **Outlier**: A value that is unusually far from most others.
- **Scatterplot**: A graph that shows the relationship between two variables.
- **Bar graph**: A graph used to compare values across categories.
- **Histogram**: A graph that shows the distribution of numerical scores.
