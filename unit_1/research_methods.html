Psychology Research Methods

A Comprehensive Guide for PSY-132 Students

1. Core Research Categories

Descriptive

Goal: Describe human behavior as it exists.

Key Tools:

  • Case Study: Individual focus.
  • Naturalistic Observation: Real-world settings.
  • Survey: Broad self-reporting.
  • Archival: Using existing records.

Correlational

Goal: Identify mathematical relationships.

Logic:

  • Passive Observation: No interference.
  • Variable Relationship: Does A move with B?
  • Limitation: Correlation does not prove causation.

Experimental

Goal: Determine cause and effect.

Components:

  • Independent Variable: The cause (manipulated).
  • Dependent Variable: The effect (measured).
  • Control Group: The baseline (no treatment).
  • Random Assignment: Crucial for true experiments.

3. Practice Application

Scenarios for practice with psychological research methods and their rationales.
Research Scenario Selected Method Rationale
A psychologist follows the same group of 50 infants from birth until age 18 to track personality development. Longitudinal The study describes change within the same individuals over an extended period.
Researchers measure the height and social confidence levels of 500 adults to see if they move in the same direction. Correlational Variables are passively measured to find a link; no variables are manipulated.
Students are randomly assigned to either a "quiet room" or a "noisy room" to test if noise affects test scores. Experimental Uses random assignment and manipulation of an independent variable (noise) to show cause.
A health psychologist compares current stress levels of 20-year-olds, 40-year-olds, and 60-year-olds simultaneously. Cross-sectional Compares different population segments at a single point in time.

Quick Terminology Guide

Independent Variable (IV): The variable that is changed or controlled in a scientific experiment to test the effects on the dependent variable.

Dependent Variable (DV): The variable that is being tested and measured; it changes based on the independent variable.

Random Assignment: Using chance procedures to ensure that each participant has the same opportunity to be assigned to any given group.

Causation: The demonstration that one variable specifically causes a change in another. Only achievable through Experimental methods.
