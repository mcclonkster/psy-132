# Unit 3: Learning & Memory

## Topics in this unit

## Topic 10: Learning (media + decision literacy)

1. **Learning** is a *relatively lasting* change in **behavior** or **knowledge** that comes from **experience**, and it can happen with or without *conscious intention*.
2. **Classical conditioning** is **learning by association**, so a *previously neutral* cue can trigger an **automatic response** when it *reliably predicts* something important.
3. **Operant conditioning** is **learning through consequences**, so behavior tends to **increase with reinforcement** and **decrease with punishment**.
4. Consequences shape habits most strongly when they are **timely** and **consistent**, and **variable reinforcement** can make behaviors *persist* even when rewards become *rare*.
5. **Generalization** and **discrimination** explain why learning can **spread** to similar situations or become more **precise**, which matters for *phobias, preferences, prejudice,* and *skill training*.
6. **Extinction** reduces a learned response when the cue *no longer predicts* the outcome, but **spontaneous recovery** and **context effects** mean old learning can *reappear*.

## Topic 11: Memory (media + decision literacy)

1. **Memory** involves **encoding**, **storage**, and **retrieval**, so forgetting can happen at *any stage* even when something was once learned.
2. **Working memory** is *limited*, so strategies like **chunking** and **focused attention** increase what gets **encoded** and reduce *overload*.
3. **Long-term memory** includes **explicit memory** (*conscious recall*) and **implicit memory** (*skills and priming*), which is why you can **know** something without being able to *explain* it.
4. **Retrieval** is *cue-dependent*, so **context**, **mood**, and **specific prompts** can dramatically change what you can recall *in the moment*.
5. Forgetting often reflects **interference** (*new learning disrupting old, or old disrupting new*) rather than information being **erased**.
6. **Memory** is **reconstructive**, so **suggestion** and **misinformation** can alter **recall**, which is why **confidence** is not the same thing as **accuracy**, especially for *details* and *timelines*.

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 3: Learning & Memory (media + decision literacy)

1. **Learning** explains how **experience** changes **behavior** over time, which is why **environments** and **consequences** often shape outcomes more than *good intentions* do.
2. **Classical conditioning** shows how **cues** can trigger **automatic reactions**, which helps explain *cravings*, *anxiety responses*, and *body-first reactions*.
3. **Operant conditioning** shows how **consequences** shape **habits**, so the practical question becomes what a behavior is **rewarded** by and how *reliably* that reward appears.
4. **Memory** works through **encoding**, **storage**, and **retrieval**, so forgetting often reflects *weak encoding* or *poor retrieval cues* rather than information being permanently **erased**.
5. **Working memory** is *limited*, which is why **multitasking** and *overload* reduce learning, and why **chunking**, **spacing**, and **focused practice** tend to work better than *rereading*.
6. **Memory** is **reconstructive**, so **confidence** and *vividness* can be misleading, and claims based on **recall** should be treated as **evidence** that needs *corroboration*.

# Unit 4: Cognition & Language

## Topics in this unit

- **Cognition**
- **Intelligence**
- **Language**

## Topic 12: Cognition (media + decision literacy)

1. **Cognition** is the mind-work of thinking, reasoning, problem solving, and decision making, and it depends on how we organize information using **concepts** and **schemas** (*mental “folders” for what things mean*).
2. We often categorize quickly using **prototypes** (*our “best example” of a category*), which speeds judgments but can also feed **stereotypes** and *snap assumptions*.
3. Problem solving can use an **algorithm** (*a step-by-step method that should work every time*) or a **heuristic** (*a fast shortcut*) that trades *accuracy* for *speed*.
4. Heuristics like **availability** (*what comes to mind easily*) and **representativeness** (*what seems typical*) can **bias** risk judgments and predictions.
5. **Mental set** and **functional fixedness** can block better solutions by locking you into *familiar patterns*, even when better options are available.
6. Biases like **confirmation bias** and **anchoring** show how prior beliefs and the *first number you hear* can steer choices, so good reasoning includes deliberately checking *alternatives* and **evidence**.

## Topic 13: Intelligence (media + decision literacy)

1. **Intelligence** broadly refers to learning from experience, solving problems, and adapting to new demands, but it is *not a single simple trait*.
2. Some theories emphasize a **general ability** while others emphasize **multiple specific abilities**, and those choices shape what tests claim to measure.
3. Standardized **IQ tests** compare performance to same-age **norms**, so an IQ score describes your **relative position** in a group, not your *worth* or your *future*.
4. A test must be **reliable** (*gives consistent results*) and **valid** (*measures what it claims*), and **reliability** alone does not guarantee a meaningful interpretation.
5. Intelligence reflects both **heredity** and **environment**, so genes influence potential while **education**, **nutrition**, **stress**, and **opportunity** shape how abilities develop and show up.
6. Real **assessment** looks for patterns of **strengths** and **needs** across skills, because someone can be highly capable overall while needing specific **supports** in specific areas.

## Topic 14: Language (media + decision literacy)

1. **Language** is a **rule-governed system** for communicating meaning, including sound patterns, word parts, sentence structure, and shared meanings that listeners interpret in **context**.
2. Children acquire language rapidly through both learning and **biological preparedness**, which is why **imitation** and **reinforcement** matter but do not fully explain grammar development.
3. Evidence suggests a **sensitive period** when language learning is easier, and severe deprivation cases like **Genie** highlight both **plasticity** and **limits**, though these cases include *confounds* that make simple conclusions *unsafe*.
4. Language supports **thinking** by giving labels and structure to ideas, and it can shape attention and memory in *subtle ways* (as suggested by **linguistic relativity**).
5. Meaning is not only in words, because **tone**, **timing**, and **nonverbal cues** can change how messages are interpreted and how people respond.
6. Language relies on **brain systems**, so injuries can disrupt **production** or **comprehension** (**aphasia**) in ways that reveal how *complex* communication really is.

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 4: Cognition & Language (media + decision literacy)

1. **Cognition** is how we represent and work with information, and tools like **concepts** and **schemas** make thinking *efficient* while also creating predictable **blind spots**.
2. **Heuristics** help us decide quickly *under uncertainty*, but they can also produce **systematic errors** like **availability**, **anchoring**, and **confirmation bias** that distort risk judgments and predictions.
3. Problem solving improves when we notice **mental set** and **functional fixedness**, because the first idea that feels obvious is often a limit we accidentally *invented*.
4. **Intelligence theories** and **testing** shape high-stakes decisions in schools and workplaces, so interpreting scores requires **careful context** and clarity about what a score does *not* measure.
5. **Measurement quality** matters here as much as anywhere else, because **reliability** and **validity** determine whether a test result is **useful information** or just *consistent noise*.
6. **Language** is a structured system for **meaning-making**, and because meaning depends on **context**, **tone**, and *shared assumptions*, communication failures are often predictable outcomes rather than personal defects.
