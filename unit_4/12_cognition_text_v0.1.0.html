Why It Matters: Thinking and Intelligence

What is the best way to solve a problem? How does a person who has never seen or touched snow in real life develop an understanding of the concept of snow? How do young children acquire the ability to learn language with no formal instruction? Psychologists who study thinking explore questions like these and are called cognitive psychologists.

Thinking has captivated people for centuries, as seen in José Ferraz de Almeida Júnior’s "Girl with a Book", Rodin’s "The Thinker", and Shi Ke’s "Huike Thinking".

What is Cognition?

Simply put, cognition is thinking, and it encompasses the processes associated with perception, knowledge, problem solving, judgment, language, and memory. Scientists who study cognition are searching for ways to understand how we integrate, organize, and utilize our conscious cognitive experiences without being aware of all of the unconscious work that our brains are doing.

Learning Objectives

  • Distinguish between concepts and prototypes
  • Explain the difference between natural and artificial concepts
  • Describe problem solving strategies, including algorithms and heuristics
  • Explain some common roadblocks to effective problem solving

Categories and Concepts

A category is a set of objects that can be treated as equivalent in some way. The mental representations we form of categories are called concepts. You use concepts to see the relationships among the different elements of your experiences and to keep the information in your mind organized and accessible.

Natural and Artificial Concepts

Natural concepts are created “naturally” through your experiences and can be developed from either direct or indirect experiences. For example, your concept of "snow" if you live in Vermont vs. if you live in the Caribbean.

An artificial concept is defined by a specific set of characteristics. Geometric shapes like squares and triangles are useful examples. A triangle always has three angles and three sides.

Prototypes

A prototype is the best example or representation of a concept. If your first pet was a Golden Retriever, there is a good chance that this would be your prototype for the category of dogs.

Schemata

A schema is a mental construct consisting of a cluster or collection of related concepts. When a schema is activated, the brain makes immediate assumptions about the person or object being observed.

  • Role Schema: Makes assumptions about how individuals in certain roles (like a firefighter) will behave.
  • Event Schema (Cognitive Script): A set of behaviors that can feel like a routine, such as walking into an elevator and turning to face the door.

Solving Problems

A problem-solving strategy is a plan of action used to find a solution. Different strategies have different action plans associated with them.

Method Description Example
Trial and error Continue trying different solutions until problem is solved Restarting phone to fix a malfunction
Algorithm Step-by-step problem-solving formula Instruction manual for software installation
Heuristic General problem-solving framework; mental shortcut Working backwards; breaking a task into steps

Cognitive Development (Piaget)

Jean Piaget focused on children’s cognitive growth. He believed that thinking is a central aspect of development and that children do not think and reason like adults.

Classifying Intelligence

Charles Spearman believed intelligence consisted of one general factor, called g. Raymond Cattell later divided general intelligence into two components:

  • Crystallized intelligence: Acquired knowledge and the ability to retrieve it.
  • Fluid intelligence: The ability to see complex relationships and solve problems.

Triarchic Theory of Intelligence

Robert Sternberg sees intelligence as comprised of three parts:

Practical
Street smarts/Common sense

Creative
Imaginative solutions

Analytical
Academic problem solving

Measuring Intelligence (IQ)

IQ stands for intelligence quotient. The average score is 100. Standard deviations (usually 15 points) describe how scores are dispersed.

Flynn Effect

The Flynn effect refers to the observation that each generation has a significantly higher IQ than the previous generation, though this doesn't necessarily mean we are more "intelligent" per se.

Learning Disabilities

Learning disabilities are cognitive disorders that affect specific areas of cognition like language or reading. They are distinct from intellectual disabilities.

  • Dysgraphia: Difficulty writing legibly; struggle putting thoughts on paper.
  • Dyslexia: Most common; inability to correctly process letters and sound-letter correspondence.
  • Dyscalculia: Difficulty learning or comprehending arithmetic.

Introduction to Language

Language is a communication system that involves using words and systematic rules to organize those words to transmit information. While many species communicate, many people assert that language makes humans unique.

Components of Language

Lexicon: The vocabulary of a given language.
Grammar: Rules used to convey meaning through lexicon.
Phoneme: Basic sound unit (e.g., "ah" vs "eh").
Morpheme: Smallest units that convey meaning.

We use semantics (deriving meaning) and syntax (word organization) to construct sentences.

Language Development

B.F. Skinner proposed that language is learned through reinforcement. Noam Chomsky criticized this, asserting that language acquisition mechanisms are biologically determined. Proficiency is maximal early in life (critical period).

The Case of Genie

Found at age 13 after extreme neglect, Genie was unable to speak. While she amassed a large vocabulary after rescue, she never mastered grammatical aspects, supporting the idea of a critical period for language acquisition.

Language and Thinking

The Sapir-Whorf hypothesis proposes that language determines thought. While researchers now view this as too absolute, language habits certainly encourage habits in thought.

"Habits in language encourage habits in thought." — Boroditsky (2001)

Examples include English vs Mandarin Chinese descriptions of time (horizontal vs vertical) and "pronoun drop" in Japanese being linked to collectivistic values.

The Social Brain & Gossip

Approximately 60%–70% of everyday conversation is gossip. Robin Dunbar argued that gossip is the human equivalent of grooming in primates—an act of socializing and signaling importance.

The social brain hypothesis suggests that human brain evolution was driven by the need to maintain larger social groups (ingroups).
