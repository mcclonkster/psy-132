# Unit 1: Foundations

## Topics in this unit

## Topic 1: History (media + decision literacy)

1. Psychology became a scientific discipline when it committed to systematic observation and experiments as the best way to test claims about mind and behavior.
2. Early approaches like introspection show a permanent problem in human knowledge: self-report can be valuable, but it needs careful methods such as standardized questions and consistent scoring because experiences are private and easy to interpret differently.
3. The big historical schools of thought matter because each one is a different answer to “What counts as an explanation?” and that question still drives disagreement in psychology today.
4. Behaviorism pushed psychology toward observable behavior and learning principles, which is why behavior is often easier to measure consistently in controlled settings than intentions, motives, or private experiences.
5. The cognitive revolution showed that invisible processes like attention and memory can be studied scientifically when we use careful methods and measurable outcomes.
6. The overall story of psychology’s history is a pattern of ideas being revised or replaced when better evidence and better methods appear.

## Topic 2: Theoretical Perspectives (media + decision literacy)

1. A theoretical perspective is a lens for explaining behavior, and different lenses emphasize different causes, evidence, and interventions.
2. The biological perspective explains behavior through the brain, nervous system, genes, and hormones, which helps evaluate claims about medication, sleep, addiction, and brain-based explanations.
3. The behavioral perspective explains behavior through learning and the environment, which helps predict change by tracking reinforcement, punishment, and what behavior is actually rewarded.
4. The cognitive perspective explains behavior through mental processes, which helps explain why the same event can lead to different reactions depending on interpretation and attention.
5. The social and personality perspective explains behavior through situations, groups, and traits, which helps avoid blaming everything on character or everything on peer pressure.
6. Strong explanations often require more than one lens, because single-cause stories tend to miss important contributing factors.

## Topic 3: Research Methods (media + decision literacy)

1. Scientific thinking starts by separating a claim from the evidence offered to support it.
2. Research designs set the limits of what can be concluded: descriptive designs describe, correlational designs relate, and experiments can test cause-and-effect.
3. Correlation cannot prove causation because a relationship can be produced by a third variable, reversed direction, or biased measurement.
4. Experiments support causal conclusions by manipulating an independent variable, measuring a dependent variable, and comparing outcomes to an appropriate control condition while using random assignment and other design features to reduce alternative explanations.
5. Every study depends on operational definitions, because what researchers choose to measure determines what their results actually mean.
6. Before trusting a study headline, the key questions are “How was this measured?” “Who was studied?” and “What other explanations were ruled out?”

## Topic 4: Statistics (media + decision literacy)

1. Statistics helps decide whether an apparent pattern is signal or noise, especially when data vary from person to person.
2. Thinking in distributions (spread and shape) prevents mistakes that happen when people rely only on a single average number.
3. Claims about a population depend on sampling, because a biased sample can produce tidy numbers that do not generalize.
4. A p-value is not “the probability the claim is true,” because it describes how surprising the data would be if the null explanation (no real effect or relationship) were true and chance were operating in the way the model assumes.
5. Effect size answers “How big is the difference or relationship?” which is often more meaningful than whether a result crosses a significance threshold.
6. Sound conclusions depend on measurement quality, so researchers care about reliability (consistency) and validity (measuring what you mean), and one does not guarantee the other.

## Topic 5: Ethics (media + decision literacy)

1. Ethics in psychology protects people from harm while allowing research and practice to produce useful knowledge.
2. Informed consent requires that participants understand what will happen, what the risks are, and that participation is voluntary and can stop without penalty.
3. Confidentiality matters because psychological data can affect reputation, relationships, schooling, and employment if mishandled.
4. Oversight systems like an Institutional Review Board (IRB) exist because researchers have incentives to find results, and participants can be vulnerable to pressure or risk.
5. Historical abuses like the Tuskegee Syphilis Study show how power and discrimination can corrupt research, which is why safeguards and accountability exist.
6. Ethical judgment requires noticing power imbalances and conflicts of interest, and asking who benefits, who is exposed to risk, and what protections are actually in place.

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 1: Foundations (media + decision literacy)

1. Psychology is most useful when it treats claims about behavior and mental life as testable rather than as intuition or tradition.
2. Different theoretical perspectives are lenses that spotlight different causes, so good thinking compares lenses instead of committing to a single story too early.
3. Research methods determine what you can conclude, which is why the first question to ask about any headline is “What kind of study was this?”
4. Statistical reasoning keeps you from being misled by tidy numbers, because significance is not the same as importance, averages can hide variability, and effect size and generalizability determine what a result means in the real world.
5. Ethical research is not a paperwork detail, because informed consent, confidentiality, and oversight exist to protect people from predictable harms and power imbalances.
6. The core habit of Unit 1 is evaluating claims by checking definitions, measurement, design, and alternative explanations before letting the conclusion steer beliefs or choices.
