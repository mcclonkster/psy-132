# Unit 2: Biology of Behavior

## Topics in this unit

## Topic 6: Neuroscience (media + decision literacy)

1. The nervous system communicates through neurons that send electrical signals (action potentials) and chemical messages across synapses using neurotransmitters.
2. Many drugs and mental health treatments change behavior by changing neurotransmission, which is why brain chemistry explanations can be partly true but rarely the whole story.
3. The nervous system includes the central nervous system (brain and spinal cord) and the peripheral nervous system, and the peripheral system includes the somatic system and the autonomic system (sympathetic and parasympathetic).
4. The endocrine system uses hormones carried in the bloodstream to influence the body more slowly than neurons do, and stress responses often involve both systems working together.
5. Brain areas show patterns of specialization, but most complex behavior comes from networks interacting, which is why one-symptom, one-area explanations usually overreach.
6. Brain imaging can provide useful evidence, but strong neuroscience avoids overclaiming by separating correlation from causation and looking for replication and converging methods.

## Topic 7: Sensation (media + decision literacy)

1. Sensation is detecting physical energy with receptors, and perception is how the brain organizes and interprets that input into an experience.
2. Sensory systems rely on transduction, where receptors convert light, sound, chemicals, pressure, or temperature into neural signals the brain can process.
3. Sensory sensitivity has limits, including absolute thresholds and difference thresholds (Weber’s law), so not noticing can be a normal feature of biology.
4. Signal detection theory shows that noticing a stimulus depends on both sensory strength and decision factors like attention, expectations, fatigue, and motivation.
5. Sensory adaptation reduces responsiveness to constant stimulation, which helps the brain highlight change and novelty.
6. Sensory systems depend on specialized receptor cells and pathways, and injury or overload can change what a person can detect and can also increase sensitivity to stimulation.

## Topic 8: Perception (media + decision literacy)

1. Perception is constructive, meaning the brain infers a best-guess experience from incomplete input, and illusions reveal the brain’s rules for interpreting the world.
2. Perception uses both bottom-up processing (data from the senses) and top-down processing (knowledge, context, and expectations), and either one can help or mislead.
3. Attention is selective, so what you notice is shaped by goals and mental workload, and missed details are often the default rather than a personal flaw.
4. Gestalt principles describe how the brain groups elements (proximity, similarity, continuity, closure, and figure–ground) to form coherent objects and scenes.
5. The brain infers depth using binocular and monocular cues, so your 3-D experience is an interpretation of 2-D images on the retinas.
6. Perception is often multimodal, meaning sight, sound, touch, and body position combine, and conflicts between senses can shift what you experience.

## Topic 9: Consciousness (media + decision literacy)

1. Consciousness includes awareness of self and environment, but it also includes automatic processes that influence thought and behavior outside awareness.
2. Biological rhythms like the circadian rhythm are coordinated by brain systems (including the suprachiasmatic nucleus) and hormones (like melatonin), so timing affects mood, alertness, and performance.
3. Sleep cycles through stages of NREM and REM, and sleep supports learning, memory consolidation, emotion regulation, and physical health.
4. Sleep loss impairs attention, reaction time, and judgment, and common sleep disorders like insomnia and sleep apnea can mimic or worsen mental health symptoms.
5. Psychoactive drugs alter consciousness by changing brain signaling, and repeated use can change the body and brain in ways that shape tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal.
6. Altered states such as dreaming, hypnosis, and meditation show that experience can change dramatically without external changes in the world, which is why subjective certainty is not the same thing as accuracy.

## Unit-level six sentences

## Unit 2: Biology of Behavior (media + decision literacy)

1. Biological explanations are powerful when they describe mechanisms, but they become misleading when they pretend the brain is the only cause of behavior.
2. Neurons, synapses, neurotransmitters, and hormones provide the basic wiring and chemistry that many treatments and drugs act on, which helps separate plausible brain claims from marketing.
3. Sensation and perception are different processes, because the senses detect input while the brain constructs an experience, and that construction can be systematically biased.
4. Attention is selective and limited, so missed details and confident errors often come from normal cognitive limits rather than from laziness or dishonesty.
5. Sleep and circadian rhythms shape learning, mood, judgment, and health, so poor sleep can look like a motivation problem or a personality problem when it is partly a physiology problem.
6. Claims based on brain scans, “chemical imbalance” slogans, or altered states are strongest when they show converging evidence, clear limits, and replication rather than a single flashy finding.
