Psychological Disorders and Treatment – Chapters 14–15

Understanding Psychological Disorders

Why This Topic Matters

  • Psychological problems affect real people’s lives.
  • Psychology studies these problems using evidence, not stereotypes.
  • In this part of the course, we ask:
    • What counts as a psychological disorder?
    • How do we decide?
    • How can psychology help?

Set the frame for the unit.

  • Acknowledge that mental health is personally relevant for many students.
  • Emphasize two themes:
    • Understanding, not judging.
    • Using research evidence, not rumors or media clichés.
  • You do not need to introduce many technical terms yet; this slide is about purpose and safety.

What We Are Studying

  • Not every problem in life is a disorder.
  • We focus on patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that:
    • Are very intense or extreme.
    • Are long-lasting.
    • Are hard to control and live with.

Connect to the concept of psychopathology.

  • Introduce psychopathology as the scientific study of mental, emotional, and behavioral disorders.
  • Define a psychological disorder in student-friendly terms:
    • A pattern of thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that causes serious distress and/or seriously interferes with daily life.
  • Make the distinction between everyday stress and disorder-level patterns based on intensity, duration, and impact.

How We Decide

  • There is no single “abnormal” test.
  • Psychologists do not just ask “Is this weird?”
  • Instead, they look for several clues:
    • How unusual something is.
    • How much it breaks social rules.
    • How much distress it causes.
    • How much it interferes with life.

Introduce the framework for thinking about “abnormal.”

  • Explain that the textbook organizes these clues into four ideas:
    • Statistical abnormality
    • Social nonconformity
    • Subjective discomfort
    • Maladaptive behavior
  • Tell students: “We will translate these labels into questions you can actually use.”
  • Do not overload the slide with the technical terms; name them verbally here.

How Unusual Is It?

  • Ask:
    • Is this pattern very rare for most people?
    • Is it far from what is typical in this culture and age group?
  • Being different is not automatically bad.
  • Extreme patterns can still be important clues.

Tie this clue to the textbook term statistical abnormality.

  • Define statistical abnormality as behavior that is far from the average or typical range in the population.
  • Give neutral examples:
    • An extremely high level of persistent anxiety compared to most people.
    • A very low need for sleep with no tiredness.
  • Clarify that rare does not equal disordered; some rare traits (for example, exceptional talents) are not disorders.

How Much Does It Break Social Rules?

  • Ask:
    • Does this behavior strongly violate social norms?
    • Does it cause serious problems with other people?
    • Or is it simply unusual or unconventional?

Connect to the idea of social nonconformity.

  • Define social nonconformity as behavior that seriously violates social norms or expectations.
  • Give generic examples:
    • Shouting at strangers in a quiet public place.
    • Ignoring basic rules of public behavior in ways that cause conflict or risk.
  • Emphasize that some nonconformity is healthy; it becomes a concern when it is extreme and disruptive.

How Much Pain and Interference?

  • Ask:
    • How much emotional pain is there?
    • How much does this pattern get in the way of:
      • Work or school?
      • Relationships?
      • Basic self-care?
  • Little pain and little interference usually suggests no disorder.

Bring in subjective discomfort and maladaptive behavior.

  • Subjective discomfort: the person feels ongoing emotional distress about their own thoughts, feelings, or actions.
  • Maladaptive behavior: the behavior makes it very difficult to meet everyday demands (for example, school, work, caregiving).
  • Use brief, generic examples:
    • Discomfort: feeling constant, overwhelming anxiety that feels unbearable.
    • Maladaptive: missing most classes for weeks due to low mood.
  • Emphasize that distress and interference are often the strongest signals that help is needed.

Putting the Clues Together

  • To think about whether something may be a psychological disorder, we look at:
    • How unusual it is.
    • How much it breaks norms.
    • How much distress it causes.
    • How much it interferes with life.
  • These clues are guides, not rigid rules.

Map the plain-language clues to the textbook terms.

  • Unusual → statistical abnormality.
  • Breaks norms → social nonconformity.
  • Distress → subjective discomfort.
  • Interference → maladaptive behavior.
  • Re-state the idea of a psychological disorder:
    • Typically involves more than one of these clues.
    • Usually involves ongoing patterns, not just a single reaction to a bad day.
  • Invite quick student input: ask for examples of something unusual but not maladaptive, or something very interfering but not particularly rare.

How Professionals Classify Disorders

  • Professionals need a shared way to describe patterns of problems.
  • They use a manual that:
    • Lists mental disorders.
    • Describes patterns of symptoms.
    • Sets out criteria for each diagnosis.

Introduce DSM-5 and diagnosis briefly.

  • Name the manual: DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition).
  • Explain:
    • Criteria: specific symptoms and durations that must be present.
    • Diagnosis: a label given when a person’s symptoms match a set of criteria.
  • Stress from the notes:
    • DSM categories are revised as research and societal views change.
    • A diagnosis is a classification tool, not a full description of a person.
  • Mention comorbidity lightly (having more than one diagnosis at the same time) without going into detail yet.

Many Causes Working Together

  • Most psychological disorders do not have a single cause.
  • We look at:
    • The body and brain.
    • The mind and coping skills.
    • Relationships, culture, and stress.
  • These influences interact.

Connect to the biopsychosocial model and diathesis–stress.

  • Biopsychosocial model:
    • Biological factors: genetics, brain structure, brain chemistry, physical illnesses.
    • Psychological factors: learning history, thought patterns, coping skills, personality.
    • Social and cultural factors: family environment, relationships, trauma, socioeconomic conditions, cultural norms.
  • Diathesis–stress model:
    • Diathesis: a predisposition or vulnerability (for example, genetic risk, early adversity).
    • Stress: life events or ongoing pressures.
    • Risk increases when vulnerability and stress are both present.
  • Keep it conceptual. Students will revisit these ideas when specific disorders and treatments are covered.

Major Types of Disorders

Big Picture Map of Disorders

  • We group psychological disorders into broad categories.
  • Each category has a typical pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
  • Focus on the main pattern and feeling, not memorizing full criteria.

Connect directly to 16e’s structure.

  • Categories to mention:
    • Psychotic disorders
    • Mood disorders
    • Anxiety disorders
    • Anxiety-related disorders (OCD, PTSD, etc.)
    • Personality disorders
    • Childhood and neurodevelopmental disorders
  • Remind students: the goal is to recognize patterns, not to learn every symptom line by line.

Psychotic Disorders

  • Psychotic disorders involve a loss of contact with reality.
  • People may:
    • Have hallucinations (sensing things that are not there).
    • Have delusions (strong beliefs that are clearly false).
    • Show disorganized thinking or speech.

Draw from the 16e notes on psychotic disorders.

  • Define hallucinations: for example, hearing voices when no one is speaking.
  • Define delusions: for example, firmly believing one is being targeted or plotted against without evidence.
  • Introduce schizophrenia as a key psychotic disorder:
    • Often involves hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought, and negative symptoms (such as reduced emotional expression).
  • Emphasize that treatments exist and many people improve with support.

Mood Disorders

  • Mood disorders involve major disturbances in emotion.
  • Depressive disorders include:
    • Long-lasting low mood and loss of interest.
    • Changes in sleep and appetite and low energy.
    • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt.
  • Bipolar and related disorders include:
    • Shifts between depression and mania or hypomania.

Base this on 14.3.

  • Clarify that depressive disorders involve persistent, impairing sadness and related symptoms.
  • Define mania briefly:
    • Elevated or irritable mood, increased energy, less sleep, racing thoughts, risky behavior.
  • Point out that bipolar disorders can be very serious but are treatable with medication and psychotherapy.

Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders feature excessive, unrealistic fear or anxiety.
  • Anxiety interferes with daily functioning.
  • Common examples:
    • Generalized anxiety disorder (chronic, widespread worry).
    • Panic disorder (repeated panic attacks plus fear of more).
    • Specific phobias (intense fear of particular objects or situations).

Anchor these examples in students’ prior understanding of anxiety.

  • Distinguish normal anxiety from disorder-level anxiety by severity, duration, and interference.
  • Clarify each example briefly:
    • Generalized anxiety disorder: persistent worry about many topics most days.
    • Panic disorder: unexpected panic attacks plus ongoing concern or behavior changes related to them.
    • Phobias: intense fear that leads to avoidance and distress.

Anxiety-Related Disorders

  • Some disorders share anxiety features but are classified separately.
  • Examples include:
    • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).
    • Trauma- and stressor-related disorders such as PTSD.

Pull from 14.4’s anxiety-related section.

  • OCD:
    • Obsessions: recurring, unwanted thoughts or urges.
    • Compulsions: repetitive behaviors done to reduce anxiety.
  • PTSD:
    • Follows trauma.
    • Core features: intrusive memories, nightmares, avoidance, feeling “on guard” or easily startled.
  • Emphasize that, even though classified separately, these still involve anxiety and fear as central components.

Personality Disorders

  • Personality disorders involve long-standing patterns of:
    • Thinking.
    • Feeling.
    • Behaving.
  • These patterns are:
    • Inflexible across situations.
    • Cause significant distress or impairment.

Link to 14.3’s personality content.

  • Give high-level examples of patterns (without naming all specific disorders):
    • Deep and lasting mistrust of others.
    • Very unstable relationships and self-image.
    • Strong need for admiration with little empathy for others.
  • Stress that these patterns appear across many situations and over long periods of time.

Childhood and Neurodevelopmental Disorders

  • These disorders typically begin in childhood.
  • They affect:
    • Attention.
    • Learning.
    • Social communication or behavior.
  • Examples include:
    • ADHD.
    • Autism spectrum disorders.
    • Specific learning disorders.

Base this on the notes for childhood and neurodevelopmental disorders.

  • ADHD:
    • Problems with sustained attention, hyperactivity, or impulsivity beyond what is typical.
  • Autism spectrum disorders:
    • Differences in social communication.
    • Restricted or repetitive behaviors, with a wide range of presentations.
  • Specific learning disorders:
    • Significant, persistent difficulty in reading, writing, or math, despite opportunity and effort.

Thinking About “Normal” and “Disordered”

  • Everyone feels anxious, sad, or stressed sometimes.
  • A disorder-level problem is usually:
    • More intense.
    • More persistent.
    • More interfering.
  • We ask:
    • How severe?
    • How long?
    • How much does it interfere with life?

This slide consolidates Section 2 of the notes.

  • Encourage students to apply the earlier “four clues” and these questions when they think about disorder categories.
  • Emphasize that diagnosis is a structured process guided by criteria; it is not about casually labeling people.

Treatments, Ethics, and Stigma

Why Treatments Matter

  • Psychological disorders can cause real suffering.
  • Many treatments are effective.
  • Goals of treatment include:
    • Reducing symptoms.
    • Improving daily functioning.
    • Enhancing quality of life.

Connect to Chapter 15’s aim.

  • Highlight that therapy and medication are not about “fixing” personality, but about reducing distress and increasing functioning.
  • Set up the idea of evidence-based treatment as treatments tested in good studies and shown to help more than no treatment or placebo.

Psychotherapy Approaches

  • Psychotherapy is a professional relationship aimed at helping people change thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
  • Approaches can differ on:
    • Understanding vs action.
    • Therapist-led vs client-led.
    • Open-ended vs time-limited.

Summarize 15.1’s psychotherapy overview.

  • Connect to the “four characteristics” from the notes:
    • Insight-oriented vs action-oriented.
    • Directive vs nondirective.
    • Open-ended vs time-limited.
    • Individual vs group; in-person vs remote.
  • Explain that the next slides will focus on the main families of therapy, not every named variant.

Action Therapies: Behavior and Cognitive

  • Behavior therapies focus on changing maladaptive behavior using conditioning principles.
  • Cognitive therapies focus on changing distorted thinking and teaching coping skills.
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) combines both behavior and cognitive methods.

Pull from 15.2 and 15.3.

  • Behavior therapies:
    • Classical conditioning techniques: aversion therapy, systematic desensitization, exposure.
    • Operant conditioning techniques: reinforcement, shaping, token economies, time-out.
  • Cognitive therapies:
    • Beck’s cognitive therapy: addresses selective perception, overgeneralization, all-or-nothing thinking.
    • REBT (rational-emotive behavior therapy): targets rigid “musts” and “shoulds.”
  • Emphasize CBT as a widely used, well-studied example.

Insight Therapies: Humanistic and Psychodynamic

  • Humanistic therapies emphasize:
    • Personal growth and self-acceptance.
    • A supportive, accepting, and empathetic relationship.
  • Psychodynamic therapies emphasize:
    • Unconscious conflicts.
    • The influence of past relationships on current feelings and behaviors.

Align with 15.1 and 15.3.

  • Humanistic (for example, client-centered therapy):
    • Focus on empathy, genuineness, and unconditional positive regard.
    • The client leads exploration; the therapist reflects and supports.
  • Psychodynamic:
    • Based on Freud’s psychoanalysis and later modifications.
    • May use techniques like exploring recurring themes, understanding transference, and examining early experiences.
  • Clarify that both are insight-oriented: they focus on understanding, not just changing behavior.

Medical and Biological Treatments

  • Medications can reduce symptoms by changing brain chemistry.
  • Brain stimulation therapies are used for some severe, treatment-resistant conditions.
  • Psychosurgery is rare and reserved for extreme cases.

Draw from 15.4.

  • Medications:
    • Antidepressants, antianxiety drugs, mood stabilizers, antipsychotics.
    • Often combined with psychotherapy.
    • Require careful monitoring for side effects.
  • Brain stimulation:
    • Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) used especially for severe depression not helped by other treatments.
  • Psychosurgery:
    • Now rare, tightly controlled, and considered only after other treatments have failed.

Hospitalization and Community Support

  • Hospitalization provides:
    • A safe, structured setting during crises.
    • Short-term intensive treatment.
  • Community mental health programs provide:
    • Ongoing support in the community.
    • Services such as crisis intervention, education, and case management.

Continue from 15.4.

  • Explain when hospitalization may be needed:
    • Serious risk of harm to self or others.
    • Severe psychotic episodes where safety and stabilization are priorities.
  • Community mental health centers:
    • Aim to prevent or shorten hospital stays.
    • Provide long-term support for people living with mental illness.

Ethics in Mental Health Care

  • Ethical practice protects:
    • Safety
    • Rights
    • Dignity
  • Key ideas:
    • Confidentiality
    • Informed consent
    • Professional boundaries
    • Cultural responsiveness

Connect back to Section 4 of your Block 8 notes.

  • Confidentiality:
    • Information shared in therapy is private, with exceptions (serious danger, certain legal requirements).
  • Informed consent:
    • Clients are told what treatment involves, potential risks and benefits, and alternatives.
  • Boundaries:
    • The therapist–client relationship is professional; therapists avoid relationships that could exploit clients.
  • Cultural responsiveness:
    • Therapists work to understand a client’s cultural background and avoid assumptions based on their own norms.

Skills, Perseverance, and Managing Mental Health

  • Change can be slow and uneven.
  • Perseverance means continuing to use coping strategies and stay engaged with treatment.
  • You can build skills to:
    • Support your own mental health.
    • Support others in seeking help.

Based on 14.5 and 15.5.

  • Tie “perseverance” to the idea of grit:
    • Working toward goals despite obstacles and setbacks.
  • Managing mental health issues:
    • Using basic counseling skills as a peer: listening, empathy, non-judgment.
    • Knowing when and how to encourage someone to seek professional help.
  • Reinforce the message that seeking counseling is a sign of strength, not weakness.

Stigma, Culture, and Evidence

  • Stigma can:
    • Make people less likely to seek or stay in treatment.
    • Increase shame and isolation.
    • Contribute to discrimination.
  • Culture and identity shape:
    • How distress is expressed.
    • How behavior is interpreted.
    • How much people trust mental health systems.
  • Evidence can help correct myths and support fairer attitudes.

Use Section 5 of your notes.

  • Barriers to getting help:
    • Cost, limited services, time pressures, language and cultural differences, stigma.
  • Clarify what stigma is: negative attitudes and stereotypes about mental illness.
  • Culture and identity:
    • Some groups have been over- or underdiagnosed.
    • Historical mistreatment can create mistrust.
  • Evidence:
    • Most people with mental illness are not violent.
    • Many improve with appropriate treatment.
  • Emphasize that accurate, research-based information helps:
    • Replace fear with understanding.
    • Encourage supportive responses and help-seeking.
