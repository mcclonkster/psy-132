Chapter 15 Therapies

15.1 What Is Psychotherapy?

What Is Psychotherapy?

  • Psychotherapy is a structured psychological intervention.
  • It is provided by trained mental health professionals.
  • Its goal is to reduce distress and improve everyday functioning.

Psychotherapy vs Other Help

  • Psychotherapy goes beyond advice or “a good listener.”
  • It uses psychological principles and evidence-based methods.
  • It follows a clear structure, with specific goals and professional boundaries.

Brief History of Psychotherapy

  • Care moved from asylums and “moral treatment” toward modern psychotherapy.
  • Psychoanalysis (Freud) was an early model of talk-based therapy.
  • Behavior, cognitive, humanistic, family, and integrative therapies developed later.

Major Dimensions of Therapies

  • Therapies differ in whether they focus on insight or on action.
  • Therapists may take a directive or a nondirective style.
  • Therapy can be offered in individual, group, or family formats.
  • Work can be open-ended or brief and time-limited.

Common Factors in Effective Therapy

  • Effective therapy has a strong therapeutic alliance built on trust and collaboration.
  • It provides a safe, structured space to work with difficult experiences.
  • Clients learn new ways of thinking, feeling, and responding.
  • Clients practice behavior change between sessions and receive support while they do so.

Does Psychotherapy Work?

  • On average, people who receive psychotherapy do better than most people who do not receive treatment.
  • Many therapy approaches are roughly equivalent when they are delivered competently.
  • For some specific disorders, certain treatments are preferred over others.

Who Benefits Most?

  • Clients benefit more when they attend sessions, engage actively, and practice between sessions.
  • Some people improve even when they have severe symptoms or long histories.
  • Outcomes are shaped by motivation, social support, and how well therapy fits the person’s life.

15.2 Behavior Therapies”

Behavior Therapies: Big Idea

  • Behavior is learned and can be reshaped.
  • Behavior therapies use conditioning principles to change behavior.
  • They focus on observable actions and the current environment.

Classical Conditioning–Based Approaches

  • Some problem behaviors are tied to conditioned fear or anxiety.
  • Behavior therapists use repeated safe exposure without escape.
  • The goal is to reduce avoidance so that fear can decline over time.

Systematic Desensitization

  • The client and therapist build a fear hierarchy from least to most feared situations.
  • The client practices relaxation or coping skills with each step.
  • The client moves through gradual exposures until fear at each level feels manageable.

Flooding and Intensive Exposure

  • Flooding uses prolonged, high-intensity exposure to the feared situation.
  • Escape and safety behaviors are not allowed during the exposure.
  • Anxiety typically rises, peaks, and then declines naturally over time.

Exposure and Response Prevention (ERP)

  • ERP is especially used for obsessive–compulsive disorder (OCD).
  • The client is exposed to an obsessional trigger in a planned, supported way.
  • The therapist helps prevent the ritual or compulsion so anxiety can habituate.
  • Clients learn, “I can tolerate the urge without giving in.”

Operant Conditioning–Based Approaches

  • Operant methods shape behavior through its consequences.
  • Desired behaviors are increased with reinforcement.
  • Dangerous or disruptive behaviors are decreased by withholding rewards and setting safe limits.

Token Economies and Behavior Contracts

  • In a token economy, clients earn points or tokens for target behaviors.
  • Tokens can be traded for privileges or rewards.
  • Behavior contracts clearly state the expected behaviors and their consequences in writing.

Intensive Behavioral Interventions

  • Intensive behavioral interventions are often used for autistic and other neurodevelopmental conditions.
  • Skills are broken into small steps and reinforced consistently.
  • Goals include increasing communication, self-care, and social skills, and reducing dangerous acts.

Strengths and Limits of Behavior Therapy

  • Strengths include clear goals and strong evidence for phobias, OCD, and some child behavior problems.
  • Behavior therapy can feel mechanical if values and meaning are ignored.
  • Therapists must plan for skills to generalize to real-world situations.

15.3 Cognitive, CBT, and Humanistic Therapies”

Cognitive and CBT Approaches: Big Idea

  • Cognitive and CBT approaches focus on thought patterns that drive emotion and behavior.
  • They emphasize that it is not just events, but our interpretations of events, that matter.
  • The goal is to help clients identify, test, and change unhelpful beliefs.

Beck’s Cognitive Therapy

  • Clients track automatic thoughts in situations that trigger strong emotions.
  • They learn to notice cognitive distortions, such as all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing, and overgeneralizing.
  • They examine evidence for and against their thoughts and build more balanced interpretations.

Ellis’s Rational–Emotive Behavior Therapy (REBT)

  • REBT uses the A–B–C model: A is the activating event, B is the belief, and C is the emotional or behavioral consequence.
  • The therapist and client target rigid or irrational beliefs, such as “I must be perfect” or “I am worthless.”
  • Clients practice replacing these beliefs with more rational, flexible self-statements.

Core Features of CBT

  • CBT uses structured, time-limited, goal-focused sessions.
  • It combines cognitive tools with behavioral experiments and homework assignments.
  • CBT has strong evidence for treating depression, anxiety, and many other conditions.

Humanistic Therapies: Big Idea

  • Humanistic therapies view people as oriented toward growth, authenticity, and self-actualization.
  • Problems often arise when a person’s experience is invalidated or when “conditions of worth” are imposed.
  • Therapy aims to create conditions that allow healthy growth to resume.

Person-Centered Therapy (Rogers)

  • The therapist offers unconditional positive regard, or accepting the client without judgment.
  • The therapist uses deep empathy and reflective listening to understand the client’s experience.
  • Genuineness, or congruence, means the therapist is real and present, not just acting out a role.

Humanistic vs Cognitive / CBT

  • Humanistic therapies are usually nondirective and emphasize the relationship and the client’s lived experience.
  • Cognitive and CBT approaches are more directive and emphasize skills, homework, and problem-solving.
  • Many modern therapies integrate elements from both humanistic and cognitive–behavioral approaches.

15.4 Medical and Biological Therapies”

Medical / Biological Therapies: Big Idea

  • Medical and biological therapies target the brain and body directly.
  • They are often combined with psychotherapy and other supports.
  • They include medications, brain stimulation, limited surgery, and hospitalization when needed.

Antipsychotic Medications

  • Antipsychotic medications are used mainly for psychotic disorders and severe mood episodes such as mania.
  • Traditional (first-generation) antipsychotics strongly block dopamine and can cause movement-related side effects.
  • Atypical (second-generation) antipsychotics act on a broader set of receptors and may cause metabolic side effects such as weight gain and changes in blood sugar.

Antidepressants and Mood Stabilizers

  • Antidepressants, including SSRIs, SNRIs, and other classes, are used for depression, many anxiety disorders, and OCD.
  • Antidepressants often take several weeks before full benefit appears, and side effects must be monitored.
  • Mood stabilizers, such as lithium and some anticonvulsants, are key treatments for bipolar disorders.

Anti-Anxiety Medications

  • Benzodiazepines can provide fast relief of acute anxiety but carry risks of tolerance, dependence, and withdrawal.
  • Some non-benzodiazepine options, such as certain antidepressants or buspirone, may be used for longer-term anxiety treatment.
  • Anti-anxiety medications are usually most effective when used short-term and combined with skills-based therapies.

Electroconvulsive Therapy (ECT)

  • Modern ECT is done under anesthesia with controlled seizures and careful medical monitoring.
  • ECT can be effective for severe, treatment-resistant depression and some forms of catatonia.
  • Possible memory side effects are weighed against potential benefits, and ECT is used when benefits clearly outweigh the risks.

Other Brain Stimulation and Psychosurgery

  • Newer brain stimulation methods include repetitive transcranial magnetic stimulation (rTMS) and deep brain stimulation, which are used in limited, specific cases.
  • Psychosurgery is now rare, highly targeted, and considered only as a last resort.
  • Historical examples such as lobotomy highlight the need for strong ethical and scientific safeguards.

Hospitalization and Community Care

  • Hospitalization is used when there is immediate danger or severe instability that requires intensive support.
  • Goals of hospitalization include safety, stabilization, medication adjustment, and discharge planning.
  • Community mental health services include outpatient care, case management, day programs, and assertive community treatment (ACT) teams.

Integrating Biological and Psychological Treatments

  • For many conditions, the best outcomes come from combining medications with psychotherapy and social support.
  • Medications can reduce symptom intensity so that clients can participate more fully in therapy and daily life.
  • Ongoing, coordinated care aims to prevent a “revolving door” pattern of repeated crises and brief contacts.
