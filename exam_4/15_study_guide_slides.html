PSY-132 Introduction to Psychology

Chapter 12 · Personality – Study Guide

Use this section to understand what personality is, how major theories explain it, and how traits and situations interact.

What Personality Is (and Is Not)

  • Personality is a relatively stable pattern in how you think, feel, and behave over time and across situations.
  • It is different from:
    • Mood: short-term emotional states (for example, “I’m in a bad mood today”).
    • Reflexes or one-time reactions: quick responses that do not reflect your usual pattern.
  • When you think about personality, think “long-term tendencies,” not one bad day.

Humanistic View – Rogers and Growth

  • Humanistic theories focus on personal growth, self-acceptance, and living authentically.
  • Fully functioning person (Rogers):
    • Shows congruence: their self-concept matches their actual experience.
    • Is open to new experiences and willing to grow.
  • Unconditional positive regard:
    • Being valued as a person without conditions (“I care about you even when you make mistakes”).
    • Supports self-acceptance and healthier development.
  • Conditional regard (“I only value you if you succeed”) can create anxiety and self-criticism.

Trait Views – Big Five and Beyond

  • Trait theories describe personality in terms of basic dimensions.
  • Big Five (OCEAN):
    • Openness (curious vs conventional)
    • Conscientiousness (organized vs careless)
    • Extraversion (outgoing vs reserved)
    • Agreeableness (kind vs suspicious)
    • Neuroticism (emotionally reactive vs calm)
  • Traits are continuous: most people are somewhere in the middle, not all-or-nothing.
  • Agreeableness in particular:
    • Tendency to be kind, trusting, and cooperative.
    • Useful when thinking about how people handle conflict and relationships.
  • Newer models like HEXACO add Honesty–Humility, which covers sincerity, fairness, and lack of greed.

Social-Cognitive View – Beliefs and Environments

  • Social-cognitive theories emphasize:
    • How you think about yourself and the world.
    • How you learn from experience.
  • Locus of control:
    • Internal: “My actions influence outcomes.”
    • External: “Things mostly happen because of luck, fate, or powerful others.”
    • Your locus of control affects motivation and persistence.
  • Reciprocal determinism (Bandura):
    • Your behavior, personal factors (beliefs, emotions), and environment all influence each other.
    • Example: being shy (personal factor) → avoiding parties (behavior) → having fewer social opportunities (environment) → reinforcing shyness.

Person–Situation Interaction

  • Trait theories and social-cognitive views come together in the person–situation interaction idea:
    • Traits influence how you usually respond.
    • Situations can pull out different sides of you.
  • It is not “all traits” or “all situations.” Behavior is shaped by both.
  • Example:
    • A generally conscientious person may still miss assignments during a major crisis.
    • A generally shy person might participate more in a small, supportive group.

Traits vs Symptoms

  • Personality traits:
    • Long-term tendencies (for example, being generally organized or generally anxious).
  • Disorder symptoms:
    • Specific patterns tied to psychological disorders (for example, panic attacks, hallucinations, episodes of deep depression).
  • A person can have:
    • High conscientiousness (trait) without depression.
    • Depressive symptoms without a particular trait profile.
  • Study clue: ask “Is this a long-term style, or a pattern tied to a disorder episode?”

Psychodynamic View – Defense Mechanisms

  • Psychodynamic theories emphasize unconscious processes and conflict.
  • Defense mechanisms are automatic strategies to reduce anxiety or protect self-esteem, such as:
    • Repression (blocking painful memories)
    • Projection (seeing your own impulses in others)
    • Rationalization (making excuses to protect your self-image)
  • Short-term, these can help you cope; long-term, overuse can block honest self-understanding and growth.

Chapter 14 · Psychological Disorders – Study Guide

Use this section to understand how psychologists define disorders, what the DSM-5 is for, and how to distinguish major categories.

What Counts as a Psychological Disorder?

  • A psychological disorder is a pattern of thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that:
    • Causes significant distress, and/or
    • Interferes with daily life (school, work, relationships, self-care).
  • It is not enough that behavior is unusual or odd; psychologists look at:
    • How intense the problem is.
    • How long it lasts.
    • How much it interferes with daily functioning.

Lenses for Understanding “Abnormal”

  • Psychologists use several lenses at once:
    • Statistical unusualness: How rare is the pattern?
    • Subjective discomfort: How much distress does the person report?
    • Social nonconformity: Does it strongly violate norms?
    • Maladaptiveness: Does it seriously interfere with living?
  • Key idea: maladaptiveness and interference with life are central.
  • Being different or nonconforming is not automatically a disorder.

DSM-5 and Diagnosis

  • The DSM-5 is a manual that:
    • Lists recognized mental disorders.
    • Provides criteria (which symptoms, how many, how long, with what impact).
  • Purpose:
    • Gives a shared language for clinicians and researchers.
    • Guides assessment and treatment planning.
  • DSM-5 does not:
    • Tell you exactly how to treat every case.
    • Tell you whether a person is “good” or “bad.”

Comorbidity

  • Comorbidity means having two or more disorders at the same time.
    • Example: depression and anxiety in the same person.
  • Many real people have overlapping conditions, not just one clean diagnosis.
  • This complicates treatment and research but reflects real life.

Stress–Vulnerability (Diathesis–Stress) Model

  • Vulnerability (diathesis):
    • Genetic, biological, and early-experience factors that increase risk.
  • Stress:
    • Life events, trauma, ongoing conflict, poverty, discrimination, and other pressures.
  • The model says:
    • Disorders often appear when vulnerability and stress combine.
    • Lowering stress and increasing support can help even when vulnerability is high.

Anxiety vs Depressive Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders:
    • Have excessive, persistent fear or worry.
    • Examples: GAD, panic disorder, specific phobia.
  • Depressive disorders:
    • Have sustained low mood or loss of interest, plus other symptoms.
    • Example: major depressive disorder.
  • Depression and anxiety often co-occur but are classified separately.

Schizophrenia – Positive and Negative Symptoms

  • Schizophrenia is a psychotic disorder with:
    • Positive symptoms (added experiences):
      • Hallucinations (perceptions without external input).
      • Delusions (strongly held false beliefs).
    • Negative symptoms (losses or reductions):
      • Flat affect (reduced emotional expression).
      • Very little speech (alogia).
      • Reduced motivation and social engagement.
  • Knowing the difference helps you understand the variety of experiences within the same diagnosis.

Personality Disorders

  • Personality disorders are:
    • Long-standing, inflexible patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving.
    • Patterns that deviate from cultural expectations and cause problems.
  • They differ from:
    • Normal personality variation (e.g., being shy or bold).
    • Short-term mood shifts.

Chapter 15 · Psychological Therapies – Study Guide

Use this section to keep straight what psychotherapy is, how approaches differ, and how medical treatments fit in.

What Psychotherapy Is For

  • Psychotherapy is a psychological treatment that:
    • Uses conversation, practice, and psychological techniques.
    • Aims to change thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
    • Reduces distress and improves functioning.
  • It is different from:
    • Casual advice from friends.
    • Purely medical procedures or medications.

Psychodynamic Therapy

  • Focus:
    • Unconscious conflicts and early experiences.
  • Common methods:
    • Free association: say whatever comes to mind.
    • Dream interpretation: look for themes and symbols.
  • Goal:
    • Insight into hidden patterns that might be driving current problems.

Client-Centered (Person-Centered) Therapy

  • Developed by Carl Rogers.
  • Style:
    • Nondirective: the therapist does not take over or give lots of advice.
  • Core conditions:
    • Empathy: understanding the client’s inner world.
    • Genuineness: being real, not fake or distant.
    • Unconditional positive regard:
      • Accepting the person even when you do not approve of all behaviors.
      • Helps clients move toward self-acceptance and growth.

Behavior Therapies – Exposure

  • Focus on observable behavior.
  • Exposure therapy:
    • Helps people face feared situations or objects instead of avoiding them.
    • Anxiety usually spikes at first and then gradually decreases if the person stays in the situation.
  • Avoidance keeps fear alive; exposure allows the nervous system to learn “I can handle this.”

Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)

  • CBT works on both thoughts and behaviors.
  • Typical CBT steps:
    • Identify automatic thoughts (“I’m a total failure”).
    • Recognize cognitive distortions (all-or-nothing thinking, catastrophizing).
    • Challenge and replace them with more balanced thoughts.
    • Add behavioral actions (practice skills, gradually face feared situations, increase healthy activities).
  • CBT is:
    • More structured and time-limited than many other therapies.
    • Widely used for depression, anxiety, and more.

Biomedical Treatments

  • Biomedical therapies use medications and medical procedures.
  • Examples:
    • Antidepressants for depression and some anxiety disorders.
    • Antipsychotics for psychotic disorders like schizophrenia.
    • Mood stabilizers for bipolar disorders.
  • Medications aim at brain chemistry, while psychotherapy aims at thoughts, feelings, behavior, and relationships.
  • Often, combined treatment (meds + therapy) works best.

Therapeutic Alliance and Help-Seeking

  • Therapeutic alliance:
    • The quality of the working relationship between client and therapist.
    • Includes trust, collaboration, and agreement on goals.
    • Strong alliance is linked to better outcomes across many therapy types.
  • Help-seeking:
    • Consider talking to a professional if:
      • Problems with sleep, appetite, mood, or functioning are persistent and disruptive.
      • Self-help strategies have not been enough.
    • Reaching out earlier can make problems easier to treat.

Group Therapy Basics

  • Group therapy:
    • Several clients meet together with one or more therapists.
    • Benefits:
      • Realizing “I am not the only one” (universality).
      • Getting feedback and support from peers.
      • Practicing new skills in a social setting.
    • Trade-offs:
      • Less privacy than one-on-one therapy.
      • Different group dynamics to manage.

Chapter 17 · Social Psychology – Study Guide

Use this section to understand how and why people connect, help, conform, and sometimes harm or exclude others.

Need to Affiliate and Belong

  • Humans have a basic need to affiliate:
    • We seek connections to reduce anxiety, feel supported, and be part of a group.
  • Social comparison:
    • We judge our abilities and opinions by comparing ourselves with others.
    • This can help us feel “normal” or motivate us to change.

Prosocial Behavior

  • Prosocial behavior:
    • Actions intended to benefit others (helping, sharing, comforting).
  • Key idea: focus on intention to help, not just outcome.
  • Motives can be:
    • Egoistic (to feel good, avoid guilt, look good).
    • Altruistic (out of concern for others, without expecting reward).
  • Empathy often encourages altruistic helping.

Bystander Effect and Responsibility

  • Bystander effect:
    • People are less likely to help when more bystanders are present.
  • Diffusion of responsibility:
    • Each person assumes someone else will step in.
  • Helping usually requires:
    • Noticing the situation.
    • Interpreting it as an emergency or needing help.
    • Feeling responsible.
    • Knowing what to do.
    • Deciding to act.
  • Anything that disrupts these steps can block helping.

Prejudice vs Discrimination

  • Prejudice:
    • Negative attitude or feeling toward a group.
  • Discrimination:
    • Negative behavior toward group members.
  • It is useful to separate:
    • What people believe (stereotypes).
    • How they feel (prejudice).
    • How they act (discrimination).
  • The Jane Elliott exercise (blue-eyes/brown-eyes) is a classic example used to highlight how attitudes and behaviors can change in a short time when group statuses shift.

Attraction – Proximity

  • Proximity (physical closeness) increases the chance of forming relationships.
    • More contact → more familiarity → greater liking (usually).
  • It partly explains why:
    • Friends and partners often come from the same school, workplace, or neighborhood.

Conformity

  • Conformity:
    • Adjusting behavior or beliefs to match group norms or expectations.
  • It is different from:
    • Obedience: following direct orders from an authority.
    • Prosocial behavior: helping.
    • Prejudice/discrimination: bias and unfair treatment.
  • Conformity can:
    • Reduce conflict and help groups function.
    • Also support harmful norms if the group is doing something wrong.
