## Chapter 12 · Personality  
### What Personality Means

- Personality is a **relatively stable pattern** of thinking, feeling, and behaving.
- It describes how you **tend** to respond across many situations.
- It is about **long-term style**, not one bad day.

---

## Chapter 12 · Personality  
### What Personality Is Not

- Personality is **not the same as mood**, which changes from day to day.
- It is **not a reflex** or one-time reaction.
- When you study examples, ask if the pattern is **ongoing** or **momentary**.

---

## 12 · Humanistic View  
### Rogers and Growth

- Humanistic theories focus on **growth, choice, and meaning**.
- A **fully functioning person** lives in a way that fits their real values and experiences.
- They are open to new experiences and willing to change.

---

## 12 · Humanistic View  
### Congruence and Regard

- **Congruence** means your self-concept matches your actual experience.
- **Unconditional positive regard** means being valued as a person even when you make mistakes.
- Conditional acceptance can push people toward **self-criticism** and hiding.

---

## 12 · Trait Views  
### Big Five Traits

- The **Big Five** describe personality using five major dimensions.
- Each trait is a **continuum**, not an all-or-nothing category.
- People have unique **profiles** across these dimensions.

---

## 12 · Trait Views  
### Agreeableness as an Example

- **Agreeableness** involves kindness, trust, and cooperativeness.
- Highly agreeable people tend to **avoid conflict** and support others.
- Lower agreeableness can show as **skepticism or competitiveness**.

---

## 12 · Social-Cognitive View  
### Locus of Control

- **Locus of control** is a belief about who or what controls outcomes.
- An **internal locus** emphasizes your own actions and effort.
- An **external locus** emphasizes luck, fate, or powerful others.

---

## 12 · Social-Cognitive View  
### Reciprocal Determinism

- Your **behavior**, your **thoughts and feelings**, and your **environment** all influence each other.
- A shy person might avoid parties, which reduces chances to practice social skills.
- Over time, these loops can **strengthen patterns** in personality.

---

## 12 · Person–Situation Interaction  
### Traits and Situations Together

- Traits shape how you **usually** behave.
- Situations can **pull out different sides** of you.
- Personality is best understood as **traits interacting with situations**.

---

## 12 · Traits vs Symptoms  
### Long-Term vs Episode

- A **trait** is a long-term tendency, such as being generally organized.
- A **symptom** is tied to a possible disorder, such as a panic attack.
- Ask whether a description sounds **ongoing** or **episodic**.

---

## 12 · Psychodynamic View  
### Defense Mechanisms

- Defense mechanisms are **unconscious strategies** that reduce anxiety.
- Examples include **repression**, **projection**, and **rationalization**.
- They can help in the short term but may block honest self-understanding.

---

## Chapter 14 · Psychological Disorders  
### Big Picture

- Disorders are about **patterns**, not just single events.
- Psychologists look at **distress**, **impairment**, and **context**.
- There is no one test that instantly labels someone as “abnormal.”

---

## 14 · Defining a Disorder  
### Core Criteria

- A psychological disorder involves **significant distress** and/or **interference** with life.
- Interference can affect work, school, relationships, or self-care.
- Unusual behavior is not automatically disordered.

---

## 14 · Lenses for “Abnormal”  
### Multiple Clues

- **Statistical unusualness** asks how rare a pattern is.
- **Subjective discomfort** asks how bad it feels from the inside.
- **Social nonconformity** and **maladaptiveness** ask how behavior fits context.

---

## 14 · Maladaptiveness  
### Why It Matters

- Maladaptiveness focuses on how much a pattern **hurts functioning**.
- It asks, “Does this behavior **get in the way** of living a healthy life?”
- This lens keeps normal differences from being mislabeled as disorders.

---

## 14 · DSM-5 and Diagnosis  
### What DSM-5 Does

- The **DSM-5** lists recognized mental disorders and their criteria.
- It gives clinicians a **shared language** for describing problems.
- It helps guide assessment and treatment planning.

---

## 14 · Comorbidity and Limits  
### Real-Life Complexity

- **Comorbidity** means a person has **two or more disorders** at the same time.
- Many people do not fit perfectly into a single category.
- Diagnosis is a **useful tool**, but it is not the whole story of a person.

---

## 14 · Stress–Vulnerability  
### Diathesis–Stress Model

- People differ in **vulnerability** based on genes and early experiences.
- Stressful events can **activate** that vulnerability.
- Lowering stress and adding support can reduce the chance of problems.

---

## 14 · Anxiety and Depressive Disorders  
### Different Patterns

- **Anxiety disorders** center on persistent, excessive fear or worry.
- **Depressive disorders** center on sustained low mood or loss of interest.
- The two often overlap, but they are separate categories.

---

## 14 · Schizophrenia  
### Positive Symptoms

- Positive symptoms **add experiences**:
  - Hallucinations, such as hearing voices.
  - Delusions, such as false beliefs of persecution.
- They show a major break with **shared reality**.

---

## 14 · Schizophrenia  
### Negative Symptoms

- Negative symptoms **reduce abilities**:
  - Flat or blunted emotion.
  - Reduced speech or motivation.
- These can be harder to notice but strongly affect daily life.

---

## 14 · Personality Disorders  
### Long-Standing Patterns

- Personality disorders involve **enduring, inflexible patterns** of behavior and inner experience.
- These patterns **deviate** from cultural expectations and cause problems.
- They show up across many situations, not just in one context.

---

## Chapter 15 · Psychological Therapies  
### Big Picture

- Psychological therapies aim to **reduce symptoms** and **improve life**.
- Approaches differ in what they **focus on** and how they **work**.
- Medication and psychotherapy are often **used together**.

---

## 15 · What Psychotherapy Is

- Psychotherapy uses **psychological methods**, not just casual talk.
- It has **goals**, **structure**, and a **professional relationship**.
- It focuses on changing **thoughts, feelings, and behaviors**.

---

## 15 · Psychodynamic Therapy  
### Insight-Focused

- Psychodynamic therapy explores **unconscious conflicts** and past experiences.
- Techniques include **free association** and **dream discussion**.
- The goal is to gain insight and loosen rigid patterns.

---

## 15 · Client-Centered Therapy  
### Relationship-Focused

- Client-centered therapy is **nondirective** and emphasizes the client’s lead.
- The therapist offers **empathy** and **genuineness**.
- **Unconditional positive regard** helps clients move toward self-acceptance.

---

## 15 · Behavior Therapy  
### Changing Actions

- Behavior therapy focuses on **what people do** and on learning.
- It uses tools like **reinforcement** and **time-out** to reshape behavior.
- Exposure-based methods help reduce fears.

---

## 15 · Exposure Therapy  
### Facing Fears

- Exposure therapy involves **approaching feared situations** instead of avoiding them.
- Anxiety usually **rises, then falls** if the person stays in the situation.
- Over time, the fear response becomes **weaker**.

---

## 15 · Cognitive-Behavioral Therapy (CBT)  
### Thoughts and Behaviors

- CBT works on **unhelpful thoughts** and **unhelpful habits**.
- It challenges **cognitive distortions** such as all-or-nothing thinking.
- It adds **behavioral practice**, like behavioral activation or skill rehearsal.

---

## 15 · Biomedical Treatments  
### Medications

- Biomedical treatments use **medications** to change brain chemistry.
- Examples include **antidepressants**, **antipsychotics**, and **mood stabilizers**.
- They are often combined with psychotherapy for better results.

---

## 15 · Alliance and Help-Seeking

- The **therapeutic alliance** is the working relationship between client and therapist.
- A strong alliance includes **trust**, **safety**, and **shared goals**.
- It is reasonable to seek help if problems are **persistent** and **disruptive**.

---

## 15 · Group Therapy Basics

- Group therapy brings several clients together with a therapist.
- It can create a sense of **“I am not alone in this.”**
- It offers **peer feedback** and chances to practice new skills.

---

## Chapter 17 · Social Psychology  
### Big Picture

- Social psychology studies how we **think about**, **influence**, and **relate to** others.
- This section focuses on:
  - **Affiliation and attraction**
  - **Prosocial behavior and helping**
  - **Prejudice, discrimination, and conformity**

---

## 17 · Need to Affiliate

- People have a basic **need to belong** to groups and relationships.
- Affiliation can **reduce anxiety** and provide social support.
- It also allows **social comparison** to help us understand ourselves.

---

## 17 · Prosocial Behavior

- **Prosocial behavior** is behavior intended to **benefit others**.
- Motives can be **self-focused** or **other-focused**, but both can lead to helping.
- Empathy often pushes behavior toward more **genuinely caring** responses.

---

## 17 · Bystander Effect

- The **bystander effect** is a drop in helping when more people are present.
- **Diffusion of responsibility** makes each person feel less personally responsible.
- Clear roles and direct requests (“You, call 911”) can counter this effect.

---

## 17 · Prejudice and Discrimination

- **Prejudice** is a negative **feeling or attitude** toward a group.
- **Discrimination** is unfair **behavior** toward group members.
- Study clue: keep separate what people **believe**, **feel**, and **do**.

---

## 17 · Attraction and Proximity

- **Proximity** refers to physical closeness and frequent contact.
- Being near someone increases the chance of **friendship or attraction**.
- Many friendships form in the same **schools, workplaces, and neighborhoods**.

---

## 17 · Conformity

- **Conformity** means changing behavior or beliefs to match a group.
- It differs from **obedience**, which involves following direct orders.
- Conformity can help groups function but can also spread **harmful norms**.
