Gateways 16e – Psychological Disorders and Therapies

Slides – Psychological Disorders and Treatment

Understanding Psychological Disorders

Why This Topic Matters

  • Psychological problems affect real people’s lives in serious ways.
  • Psychology studies these problems using evidence, not stereotypes or rumors.
  • In this part of the course, we ask what counts as a psychological disorder, how we decide, and how psychology can help.

Set the frame for the unit.

  • Acknowledge that mental health is personally relevant for many students.
  • Emphasize two themes:
    • Understanding, not judging.
    • Using research evidence, not media clichés or myths.
  • This slide is about purpose and tone; you do not need to introduce many technical terms yet.

What We Are Studying

  • Not every life problem is a disorder.
  • We focus on patterns of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors that are:
    • Very intense or extreme.
    • Long-lasting over time.
    • Hard to control and live with day to day.

Connect to the concept of psychopathology.

  • Introduce psychopathology as the scientific study of mental, emotional, and behavioral disorders.
  • Define a psychological disorder in student-friendly terms:
    • A pattern of thoughts, feelings, or behaviors that causes serious distress and/or seriously interferes with daily life.
  • Distinguish everyday stress from disorder-level patterns using intensity, duration, and impact.

How We Decide

  • There is no single “abnormal” test.
  • Psychologists do not just ask “Is this weird?” or “Would I do that?”
  • Instead, they look for several clues:
    • How unusual something is.
    • How much it breaks social rules.
    • How much distress it causes.
    • How much it interferes with life.

Introduce the framework for thinking about “abnormal.”

  • Explain that the textbook organizes these clues into four ideas:
    • Statistical abnormality
    • Social nonconformity
    • Subjective discomfort
    • Maladaptive behavior
  • Tell students: “We will translate these labels into questions you can actually use.”
  • Keep the slide itself in plain language; name the technical terms verbally.

How Unusual Is It?

  • Ask whether the pattern is very rare compared to most people.
  • Ask whether it is clearly far from what is typical in this culture and age group.
  • Remember that being different is not automatically bad or disordered.

Tie this clue to the textbook term statistical abnormality.

  • Define statistical abnormality as behavior that is far from the average or typical range in the population.
  • Give neutral examples:
    • An extremely high level of persistent anxiety compared to most people.
    • Unusually low need for sleep without feeling tired.
  • Clarify that being rare does not automatically mean “disorder”; some rare traits (for example, exceptional talents) are not pathological.

How Much Does It Break Social Rules?

  • Ask whether the behavior seriously violates social norms.
  • Ask whether it causes repeated conflict or confusion with other people.
  • Consider whether it is truly disruptive, not just unconventional.

Connect this clue to social nonconformity.

  • Define social nonconformity as behavior that strongly violates social rules or expectations in a particular culture.
  • Offer generic examples:
    • Shouting at strangers in a quiet public place.
    • Ignoring basic rules of public behavior in ways that create risk or serious conflict.
  • Emphasize that some nonconformity is healthy; concern grows when behavior is extreme and damaging to functioning.

How Much Pain and Interference?

  • Ask how much emotional pain the person reports.
  • Ask how much the pattern gets in the way of:
    • Work or school responsibilities.
    • Relationships with other people.
    • Basic self-care and daily tasks.
  • Little pain and little interference usually suggests no disorder.

Bring in subjective discomfort and maladaptive behavior.

  • Subjective discomfort: the person feels ongoing emotional distress about their own thoughts, feelings, or actions.
  • Maladaptive behavior: the behavior makes it very difficult to meet everyday demands, such as attending school or work.
  • Use short examples:
    • Discomfort: constant, overwhelming anxiety or sadness that feels unbearable.
    • Maladaptive: missing most classes for weeks or neglecting hygiene because of low mood.
  • Emphasize that distress and interference are often the strongest signals that help is needed.

Putting the Clues Together

  • To think about whether something may be a psychological disorder, we look at:
    • How unusual it is.
    • How much it breaks norms.
    • How much distress it causes.
    • How much it interferes with life.
  • These clues are used as guides, not rigid rules.

Map the plain-language clues to the textbook terms.

  • Unusual → statistical abnormality.
  • Breaks norms → social nonconformity.
  • Distress → subjective discomfort.
  • Interference → maladaptive behavior.
  • Re-state the idea of a psychological disorder:
    • Typically involves more than one of these clues.
    • Usually involves ongoing patterns, not just a single reaction to a bad day.
  • Invite quick student input: examples of behavior that is unusual but not maladaptive, or very interfering but not rare.

How Professionals Classify Disorders

  • Professionals need a shared way to describe patterns of problems.
  • They use a manual that:
    • Lists mental disorders.
    • Describes patterns of symptoms.
    • Sets out criteria for each diagnosis.

Introduce DSM-5 and diagnosis briefly.

  • Name the manual: DSM-5 (Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition).
  • Explain:
    • Criteria: specific symptoms and durations that must be present.
    • Diagnosis: a label given when a person’s symptoms match a set of criteria.
  • Stress:
    • DSM categories are revised as research and societal views change.
    • A diagnosis is a classification tool, not a full description of a person.
  • Mention comorbidity (having more than one diagnosable disorder at the same time), and that DSM sometimes distinguishes positive symptoms (added experiences) from negative symptoms (losses of normal functions).

Many Causes Working Together

  • Most psychological disorders do not have a single cause.
  • We look at:
    • The body and brain.
    • The mind and coping skills.
    • Relationships, culture, and stress.
  • These influences interact over time.

Connect to the biopsychosocial model and diathesis–stress.

  • Biopsychosocial model:
    • Biological factors: genetics, brain structure, brain chemistry, physical illnesses.
    • Psychological factors: learning history, thought patterns, coping skills, personality.
    • Social and cultural factors: family environment, trauma, life stress, socioeconomic conditions, cultural norms.
  • Diathesis–stress model:
    • Diathesis: a predisposition or vulnerability (for example, genetic risk, early adversity).
    • Stress: life events or ongoing pressures.
    • Risk increases when vulnerability and stress are both present.
  • Keep it conceptual here. You will revisit these ideas when discussing specific disorders and treatments.

Major Types of Disorders

Big Picture Map of Disorders

  • Psychological disorders can be grouped into broad categories.
  • Each category has a typical pattern of thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
  • Focus on recognizing the main pattern and feeling, not memorizing every symptom.

Connect to Chapter 14’s structure.

  • Categories to highlight:
    • Psychotic disorders
    • Mood disorders
    • Anxiety disorders
    • Anxiety-related disorders (OCD, PTSD, etc.)
    • Personality disorders
    • Childhood and neurodevelopmental disorders
  • Remind students that more detailed criteria are in DSM and the textbook; slides emphasize the big picture.

Psychotic Disorders

  • Psychotic disorders involve a loss of contact with reality.
  • People may:
    • Have hallucinations (sensing things that are not there).
    • Have delusions (strong beliefs that are clearly false).
    • Show disorganized thinking or speech.

Draw from 14.2.

  • Define hallucinations (for example, hearing voices when no one is speaking).
  • Define delusions (for example, believing one is being plotted against without evidence).
  • Identify delusional disorders and schizophrenia as key psychotic disorders.
  • Note that schizophrenia often includes hallucinations, delusions, disorganized thought, and negative symptoms like reduced emotional expression.
  • Emphasize that while these disorders can be severe, treatment and support can help many people.

Mood Disorders

  • Mood disorders involve major disturbances in emotion.
  • Depressive disorders include:
    • Long-lasting low mood and loss of interest.
    • Changes in sleep and appetite and low energy.
    • Feelings of worthlessness or guilt.
  • Bipolar and related disorders include:
    • Shifts between depression and mania or hypomania.

Based on 14.3.

  • For depressive disorders, underscore persistence and interference with daily life.
  • Define mania:
    • Very elevated or irritable mood, increased energy, less need for sleep, racing thoughts, impulsive or risky behavior.
  • Explain that bipolar disorders involve episodes of depression and mania or hypomania.
  • Emphasize that mood disorders are treatable and that many people benefit from a combination of medication and psychotherapy.

Anxiety Disorders

  • Anxiety disorders feature excessive, unrealistic fear or anxiety.
  • Anxiety interferes with daily functioning.
  • Common examples:
    • Generalized anxiety disorder: chronic, widespread worry about many aspects of life.
    • Panic disorder: repeated panic attacks and persistent concern about having more.
    • Phobias: intense, irrational fear of specific objects or situations.

Align with 14.4.

  • Clarify the difference between everyday anxiety and disorder-level anxiety (severity, duration, interference).
  • Briefly unpack each example:
    • Generalized anxiety disorder: worry that is difficult to control and occurs most days for months.
    • Panic disorder: sudden surges of intense fear plus worry or behavior change about future attacks.
    • Phobias: strong fear that leads to avoidance and distress when the object or situation is encountered.

Anxiety-Related Disorders

  • Some disorders share anxiety features but are classified separately.
  • Examples include:
    • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD).
    • Trauma- and stressor-related disorders such as PTSD.

Continue from 14.4.

  • OCD:
    • Obsessions: recurring, unwanted thoughts or images.
    • Compulsions: repetitive behaviors done to reduce anxiety from obsessions.
  • PTSD:
    • Follows exposure to trauma.
    • Common features: intrusive memories, nightmares, avoidance of reminders, hyperarousal.
  • Emphasize that anxiety is central to these conditions, even though DSM-5 groups them separately.

Personality Disorders

  • Personality disorders involve long-standing patterns of:
    • Thinking.
    • Feeling.
    • Behaving.
  • These patterns are:
    • Inflexible across situations.
    • Cause significant distress or impairment.

Based on 14.3’s personality section.

  • Give high-level examples (without naming every specific diagnosis):
    • Deep mistrust of others.
    • Chronic instability in relationships and self-image.
    • Strong need for admiration with little empathy.
  • Stress that these patterns are persistent and show up across many different situations in a person’s life.

Childhood and Neurodevelopmental Disorders

  • These disorders typically begin in childhood.
  • They affect:
    • Attention.
    • Learning.
    • Social communication or behavior.
  • Examples include:
    • ADHD.
    • Autism spectrum disorders.
    • Specific learning disorders.

Summarize from neurodevelopmental content.

  • ADHD:
    • Difficulty sustaining attention, hyperactivity, and/or impulsivity beyond what is typical.
  • Autism spectrum disorders:
    • Differences in social communication.
    • Restricted or repetitive behaviors with varied severity.
  • Specific learning disorders:
    • Persistent problems with reading (dyslexia), writing, or math that are not due to lack of opportunity.

Thinking About “Normal” and “Disordered”

  • Everyone feels anxious, sad, or stressed sometimes.
  • A disorder-level problem is usually:
    • More intense.
    • More persistent.
    • More interfering.
  • We ask:
    • How severe?
    • How long?
    • How much does it interfere with life?

Consolidate the “Normal vs disordered” section.

  • Encourage students to apply:
    • The four abnormality clues.
    • These three practical questions.
  • Emphasize that the goal is careful thinking, not quick labeling.
  • Make clear that diagnosis is done by trained professionals using structured criteria.

Treatments, Ethics, and Stigma

Why Treatments Matter

  • Psychological disorders can cause real suffering and disruption.
  • Many treatments are effective when properly used.
  • Treatment aims to:
    • Reduce symptoms.
    • Improve daily functioning.
    • Enhance quality of life.

Connect to Chapter 15.

  • Highlight that treatment is not about “fixing” someone’s identity.
  • Introduce the idea of evidence-based treatment:
    • Treatments tested in well-designed studies.
    • Shown to help more than no treatment or placebo on average.

Psychotherapy Approaches

  • Psychotherapy is a professional relationship aimed at helping people change thoughts, feelings, and behaviors.
  • Approaches can differ in:
    • How much they focus on understanding vs action.
    • How much the therapist directs vs lets the client lead.
    • How long treatment is planned to last.

Summarize 15.1.

  • Relate these bullets to:
    • Insight-oriented vs action-oriented.
    • Directive vs nondirective.
    • Open-ended vs time-limited.
  • Mention that therapy can also be individual, group, couples/family, and can be in-person or remote.

Action Therapies: Behavior and Cognitive

  • Behavior therapies focus on changing maladaptive behavior using conditioning.
  • Cognitive therapies focus on changing distorted thinking and teaching coping skills.
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT) combines behavior and cognitive methods.

Draw from 15.2 and 15.3.

  • Behavior therapies:
    • Classical conditioning techniques: aversion therapy, systematic desensitization, exposure.
    • Operant conditioning techniques: reinforcement, shaping, token economies, time-out.
  • Cognitive therapies:
    • Beck’s cognitive therapy and REBT as examples of approaches targeting thought patterns.
  • Emphasize CBT as a common, evidence-based approach that blends both strategies.

Insight Therapies: Humanistic and Psychodynamic

  • Humanistic therapies emphasize:
    • Personal growth and self-acceptance.
    • A supportive, accepting, and empathetic relationship.
  • Psychodynamic therapies emphasize:
    • Unconscious conflicts.
    • The influence of past relationships on current feelings and behavior.

Based on 15.1 and 15.3.

  • Humanistic (e.g., client-centered therapy):
    • Focus on empathy, genuineness, and unconditional positive regard.
    • The client leads the conversation; the therapist reflects and supports.
  • Psychodynamic:
    • Evolved from psychoanalysis.
    • Involves exploring recurring themes and how early experiences shape current patterns.

Medical and Biological Treatments

  • Medications can reduce symptoms by changing brain chemistry.
  • Brain stimulation therapies are used for some severe, treatment-resistant conditions.
  • Psychosurgery is rare and reserved for extreme cases.

Use 15.4.

  • Medications:
    • Antidepressants, antianxiety drugs, mood stabilizers, antipsychotics.
    • Often most helpful when combined with psychotherapy.
  • Brain stimulation:
    • ECT as a example for severe depression not responsive to other treatments.
  • Psychosurgery:
    • Rare interventions, only after other options have failed and under strict controls.

Hospitalization and Community Support

  • Hospitalization provides:
    • A safe, structured setting during crises.
    • Short-term intensive treatment.
  • Community mental health centers provide:
    • Support in everyday settings.
    • Services like crisis intervention, education, and case management.

Continue 15.4.

  • Explain when hospitalization may be needed:
    • Serious risk of self-harm or harm to others.
    • Severe psychotic symptoms requiring stabilization.
  • Community services:
    • Aim to prevent or shorten hospital stays.
    • Help people live as independently as possible with support.

Ethics in Mental Health Care

  • Ethical practice protects:
    • Safety
    • Rights
    • Dignity
  • Key ideas include:
    • Confidentiality
    • Informed consent
    • Professional boundaries
    • Cultural responsiveness

Align with ethics content.

  • Confidentiality:
    • Information is kept private, except in specified risk or legal situations.
  • Informed consent:
    • Clients know what treatment involves and agree voluntarily.
  • Boundaries:
    • Therapists maintain a professional role and avoid dual relationships that could harm clients.
  • Cultural responsiveness:
    • Therapists strive to understand clients’ cultural backgrounds and adjust care accordingly.

Skills, Perseverance, and Managing Mental Health

  • Change is often slow and uneven.
  • Perseverance means continuing to work on coping and treatment, even when it is difficult.
  • You can build skills to:
    • Support your own mental health.
    • Support others in seeking help.

Based on 14.5 and 15.5.

  • Connect perseverance to the idea of grit:
    • Continuing to work toward goals despite obstacles and setbacks.
  • Managing mental health:
    • Basic peer-support skills: listening, empathy, non-judgment.
    • Normalizing help-seeking as a strength.

Stigma, Culture, and Evidence

  • Stigma can keep people from seeking or staying in treatment.
  • Culture and identity influence:
    • How distress is expressed.
    • How behavior is interpreted.
    • How much people trust mental health systems.
  • Research evidence helps correct myths and support more fair attitudes.

Use the access and stigma section of the notes.

  • Barriers to care:
    • Cost, limited services, practical constraints, language and cultural differences, stigma.
  • Define stigma: negative attitudes, stereotypes, and discrimination related to mental illness.
  • Culture and identity:
    • Some groups have been overdiagnosed or underdiagnosed.
    • Historical mistreatment can create mistrust of mental health systems.
  • Evidence shows:
    • Most people with mental illness are not violent.
    • Many improve with appropriate treatment.
  • Emphasize that accurate information supports compassion and better decisions.

14.1 Psychopathology: Classification and Causes

Psychopathology is the study of mental, behavioral, and emotional disorders.

Four things to consider when deciding whether behavior is abnormal

The textbook identifies four key ideas to think about when deciding if behavior is abnormal:

  1. Statistical abnormality
    • Behavior is rare or falls far from the average.
    • Example: very extreme scores on a depression scale compared to most people.
  2. Nonconformity (social nonconformity)
    • Behavior seriously violates social norms or expectations.
    • Example: acting in ways that are grossly inappropriate for the situation, such as shouting at strangers in a quiet library.
  3. Subjective discomfort
    • The person feels intense, ongoing emotional pain or distress about their own thoughts or behavior.
    • Example: someone feels tormented by constant, unwanted intrusive thoughts.
  4. Maladaptive behavior
    • The behavior seriously interferes with a person’s ability to function (work, school, relationships, self-care).
    • Example: someone who stops going to work, cannot maintain hygiene, or cannot manage daily tasks because of their symptoms.

Key points:

  • These four ideas are guides, not strict rules.
  • A psychological disorder usually involves more than one of these, especially subjective discomfort and maladaptive behavior.

How disorders are classified and diagnosed

  • Psychological problems are classified using the Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders, Fifth Edition (DSM-5).
  • A diagnosis is made by checking which symptoms are present and which are absent, and seeing if they match the criteria for a particular disorder.
  • People can be comorbid, meaning they show symptoms that fit criteria for more than one disorder at the same time.
  • DSM categories are revised over time as research and social norms change; categories can be added, changed, or removed.

The DSM describes symptoms in terms of:

  • Positive symptoms – behaviors or experiences that are more than normal (for example, hallucinations, delusions).
  • Negative symptoms – losses or deficits compared to normal (for example, flat affect, lack of speech).

Causes of mental illness and the biopsychosocial model

The chapter emphasizes the biopsychosocial model:

  • Biological factors: genetics, brain structure and chemistry, physical illness.
  • Psychological factors: learning history, thoughts, coping skills, personality.
  • Social and cultural factors: relationships, family environment, culture, life stress, trauma, socioeconomic conditions.

The diathesis–stress idea fits here: people may have a predisposition (diathesis) that, combined with stress, leads to a disorder.

14.2 Psychotic Disorders

Psychotic disorders involve a loss of contact with reality.

  • Delusional disorders
    • Main feature: strong, false beliefs that are not shared by others and persist despite evidence to the contrary.
    • Example: believing you are being plotted against without any real proof.
  • Schizophrenia
    • Often involves:
      • Hallucinations (most often hearing voices).
      • Delusions (paranoid or grandiose beliefs).
      • Disorganized thought and speech.
      • Negative symptoms such as reduced emotional expression or lack of motivation.
    • Typically long-term and can be very disabling, but treatment can help many people.

14.3 Mood and Personality Disorders

Mood disorders

Mood disorders involve major disturbances in emotion, such as long-lasting sadness or extreme swings.

  • Depressive disorders
    • Persistent low mood, loss of interest, changes in sleep and appetite, fatigue, feelings of worthlessness, possible thoughts of death or suicide.
  • Bipolar and related disorders
    • Shifts between depression and mania or hypomania:
      • Elevated mood.
      • Increased energy.
      • Decreased need for sleep.
      • Racing thoughts.
      • Impulsive or risky behavior.

Personality disorders

  • Long-standing patterns of thinking, feeling, and behaving that:
    • Are inflexible across situations.
    • Cause significant distress or impairment.
  • Examples include enduring patterns of:
    • Distrust.
    • Instability in relationships and self-image.
    • Strong need for admiration with lack of empathy.

14.4 Anxiety and Anxiety-Related Disorders

Anxiety disorders

  • Feature excessive, unrealistic fear or anxiety that interferes with functioning.

Examples:

  • Generalized anxiety disorder: chronic, widespread worry about many aspects of life.
  • Panic disorder: repeated panic attacks and persistent concern about having more.
  • Phobias: intense, irrational fear of specific objects or situations (for example, heights, animals).

Anxiety-related disorders

These conditions share anxiety features but are classified separately in DSM-5.

Examples include:

  • Obsessive-compulsive disorder (OCD): recurring unwanted thoughts and repetitive behaviors done to reduce anxiety.
  • Trauma- and stressor-related disorders such as PTSD:
    • Intrusive memories.
    • Nightmares.
    • Avoidance.
    • Hyperarousal following trauma.

14.5 Psychology and Your Skill Set: Perseverance

This section focuses on perseverance, often discussed in terms of “grit”:

  • Perseverance means continuing to work toward goals despite obstacles and setbacks.
  • The text highlights how sticking with treatment and coping strategies over time is important for recovery.
  • Students are encouraged to:
    • Recognize that change can be slow.
    • Practice balancing persistence with self-care.
    • Think about how to apply these ideas to their own challenges.

15.1 The Origins and Effectiveness of Psychotherapy

A brief history of psychotherapy

  • Early roots in psychoanalysis (Freud): talking about thoughts, dreams, and childhood experiences to uncover unconscious conflicts.
  • Later developments include:
    • Behavior therapies (influenced by learning theory).
    • Cognitive therapies (focused on thought patterns).
    • Humanistic therapies (focused on growth and self-acceptance).

Four characteristics that distinguish psychotherapies

Psychotherapies can be described along these dimensions:

  • Insight-oriented vs action-oriented
    • Insight-oriented: focus on understanding thoughts and feelings (for example, psychodynamic, humanistic).
    • Action-oriented: focus on changing behaviors and thoughts directly (for example, behavioral, cognitive-behavioral).
  • Directive vs nondirective
    • Directive: therapist takes an active role in guiding the client, giving advice, or assigning tasks.
    • Nondirective: therapist mainly reflects and supports, letting the client lead.
  • Open-ended vs time-limited
    • Open-ended: duration is flexible.
    • Time-limited: set number of sessions.
  • Individual vs group; in-person vs remote
    • Therapy can be one-on-one, in a group, or with couples/families.
    • It can be delivered face-to-face or via phone/Internet.

Core features of effective psychotherapy

The effectiveness of therapy depends on:

  • Client characteristics
    • Motivation, expectations, and resources.
  • Therapist characteristics
    • Warmth, empathy, experience, cultural competence.
  • Therapeutic alliance
    • The quality of the working relationship and mutual trust between client and therapist.
  • Nature of the problem
    • Some problems respond better to certain approaches than others.

Overall, research shows that psychotherapy is often more helpful than no treatment at all across many problems.

15.2 Behavior Therapies

Behavior therapies focus on changing maladaptive behavior, often using principles of conditioning.

Main elements of behavior therapies

  • Behavior therapists apply classical and operant conditioning to directly change problematic behaviors and thoughts.
  • They emphasize behavior change and skill-building more than deep “insight.”

Therapies based on classical conditioning

  • Aversion therapy
    • Pairs an unwanted behavior (for example, heavy drinking) with something unpleasant to reduce that behavior.
  • Exposure therapies
    • Help people face feared situations or cues safely, to reduce anxiety over time.
    • Types include:
      • Flooding: facing the fear all at once.
      • Systematic desensitization: gradual exposure paired with relaxation.
      • Modeling: observing others confronting fears.

These techniques aim to weaken learned fear responses.

Therapies based on operant conditioning

  • Use reinforcement and other consequences to reduce undesired behaviors and increase desired ones.

Tools include:

  • Positive reinforcement: rewarding desirable behaviors.
  • Non-reinforcement and extinction: removing rewards to reduce unwanted behaviors.
  • Punishment: adding unpleasant consequences to discourage behavior (used carefully).
  • Shaping: reinforcing small steps toward a desired behavior.
  • Stimulus control: managing cues that trigger behaviors.
  • Time-out: removing access to reinforcement after misbehavior.

Examples:

  • Token economies: people earn tokens for positive behaviors and exchange them for rewards.
  • Intensive behavioral intervention (IBI): structured behavioral programs, often used with children with autism.

15.3 Cognitive and Humanistic Therapies

Cognitive therapies

Cognitive therapies focus on distorted thinking and teach coping skills.

Key approaches:

  • Beck’s cognitive therapy
    • Targets patterns like selective perception (only seeing the negative), overgeneralization, and all-or-nothing thinking.
    • Helps clients identify and change these thought patterns.
  • Cognitive-behavioral therapy (CBT)
    • Integrates cognitive and behavioral methods.
    • Clients learn to challenge distorted thoughts and test new behaviors.
  • Rational-emotive behavior therapy (REBT)
    • Trains clients to identify irrational beliefs (“musts,” “shoulds”) and replace them with more realistic, flexible thoughts.

Overall, cognitive therapies aim to modify the thoughts that fuel distress and to build more effective coping.

Humanistic therapies

Humanistic therapies are insight-oriented:

  • They help clients gain deeper understanding of their own thoughts, emotions, and behaviors.
  • Emphasize personal growth, self-acceptance, and finding meaning.

Common elements:

  • Seeing the client as basically good and capable of growth.
  • Providing warmth, empathy, and genuine respect.
  • Letting the client lead the exploration while the therapist offers support and reflection.

15.4 Medical Therapies

Main types of medical therapies

  • Pharmacotherapy (medications)
    • Use of drugs (for example, antidepressants, antipsychotics, mood stabilizers) to change brain chemistry and reduce symptoms.
  • Brain stimulation therapies
    • Methods like electroconvulsive therapy (ECT), used especially for severe depression that has not responded to other treatments.
  • Psychosurgery
    • Rare surgical procedures on the brain for severe conditions that do not respond to other treatments; much less common now and tightly controlled.

Hospitalization and community mental health programs

  • Hospitalization
    • Provides a protected setting for intensive treatment when there is serious risk (for example, suicidal intent, severe psychosis).
  • Community mental health centers
    • Aim to prevent or reduce the need for hospitalization.
    • Offer services like education, consultation, crisis intervention, and ongoing support in the community.

15.5 Psychology and Your Skill Set: Managing Mental Health Issues

This section emphasizes skills for dealing with mental health problems in everyday life.

Key ideas include:

  • Basic counseling skills
    • Listening carefully, showing empathy, and avoiding judgment when someone shares their struggles.
    • Knowing how to encourage someone to seek professional help when needed.
  • Getting counseling
    • Understanding that seeking help is a sign of strength, not weakness.
    • Knowing steps to find support (talking to a trusted adult, contacting a counseling center, exploring community resources).

The message:

  • Many psychological problems can be helped.
  • You can play a role in supporting others and in getting help for yourself when you need it.
